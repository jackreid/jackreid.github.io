# The Tree, the Tailor, and the Traitor

Sola was still sipping on her morning *mahtar* and flipping through the notes of her most recent case when a reptilian form several meters long slithered into her office.

"Greetings, Inquirer Sola." The low sibilant voice resonated through the small, cluttered room. The tip of their tail gently pulled the office door shut.

Sola leapt to her feet in surprise. She didn't startle easily but this would do it. An adult Bayhatile stood-laid across her desk from her, its coiled form occupying most of the limited space available. It's dark green scales glistened in the morning light that streamed in through the slits of window blinds.  Sola didn't often get Bayhatile clients but it wasn't so unusual as to shake her so. What had startled her was not the large reptile but the faint red patterns painted down the length of the Bayhatile, the mark of a upper-class citizen of the Invetilic Empire. Sola was a professional though and swiftly recovered herself, rotating her head in a formal Invetlic welcome.

A slow ripple ran down the length of her visitor, an expression of pleasure that a resident of Utopia knew how to properly demonstrate respect. 

"Our name is Beesheelon." The wide oval mouth of the Bayhatile moved far more than a human mouth would to speak such a sentence and yet it did so without causing any corresponding motion elsewhere on Beesheelon's face. Sola had always found this phenomenon disconcerting. That and the fact that the mouth was large enough to swallow her whole. Her deepseated, instinctual fear didn't cause her to miss the fact that Beesheelon hadn't provided a title. Evidently they were here as a private citizen. *Curious.*

Sola sat back down. "How can I help you, neighbor?" While Beesheelon may be a representative of the Invetilic Empire, this office was in Utopia, and their conversation would be conducted accordingly. 

"We come in pursuit of a fugitive. A terrorist." 

*Fuck.* Sola had known this day would come. Utopia was an independent polity, a small anarchist station floating out in the backwaters of the Argentum Arm. It had been that way for going on some three hundred years at this point. That didn't stop neighboring states from claiming jurisdiction over Utopia, of course. At any given time there were up four such claimants. The Invetilic Empire was only the most recent such claimaint, after their conquest of the neighboring Witon Confederation eight years back led to inheriting the longstanding Witonian claim to the station. Still, most of the residents of Utopia had convinced themselves that things would continue on as always. The Invetilic would soon find that this out-of-the-way station was essentially ungovernable, just like every would be overlord had before them. Besides, there were advantages to having a place like Utopia next door. The rich and powerful of any state tended to desire certain goods that the government of their state outlawed or restricted. What could be more helpful than an established port that had no laws? As long as you didn't try to trade in sentient beings and kept your affairs nonviolent, the locals would be happy to deal with you. Let the Invetilic Empire say they were in charge. Utopia would just keep on keeping on.

Sola had always been more skeptical. The Invetilics were of a far more expansionist mindset than your average self-described empire, which was really saying something. Sooner or later they were going to try stick those prehensile tongues of theirs into Utopian affairs. Still, she had hoped that the ongoing war with the Severans on the far side of the empire would keep them busy for the foreseeable future. With a high-ranking Bayhatile in her office, however, she would need to step carefully if she wanted to avoid burning down the continuing experiment that was Utopia.

"If you are here on behalf of the honored Invetlic Empire, surely a member of the Volunteer Diplomatic Corps would be better positioned to assist you." This was utter hogwash, of course. Not only was Beesheelon clearly seeking to avoid formal channels, but the entire point of the Diplomats was to make representatives of the neighboring polities *feel* like their desires were being accommodated without *actually* doing anything meaningful. Still, playing such games was simply the way things worked for states with actual governments. They invariably couldn't comprehend how things actually worked on Utopia.

"No..." Beesheelon hissed in that monotone, overloud whisper that was the speech of Bayhatiles while making a gesture with their tongue that would have been inscrutable to most humans. "This is not a matter of statecraft. There is no treaty... to be negotiated. We... simply desire the assistance of local law enforcement in apprehending... a dangerous criminal. We desire *your* assistance, *Solaterra Trabahan Midama*." 

Oh there were layers and *layers* to that statement. Sola slowly sipped her *mahtar*, willing the caffeine into her bloodstream as she carefully considered her response. Thankfully Bayhatiles tended to be highly patient conversationalists.

For one, Sola wondered if calling her "law enforcement" was the result of a mistranslation or of a genuine misunderstanding as to her role here. Utopia was an anarchist station. It had *no* law enforcement.  Her role — a purely volunteer role, mind you — was somewhere between community mediator and private detective. People came to her if they wanted to find out what had happened or where someone was or what how to negotiate a fraught situation. This did, however, make her the closest thing on Utopia to a cop (something that her fellow citizens never let her forget), so she decided not to correct Beesheelon on this point.

The fact that Beesheelon specifically *didn't* want to involve the Diplomats — and that is certainly what that tongue flick meant, Sola wasn't *that* out of practice interacting with  Bayhatiles — was a positive sign. It meant that they (and in turn the Invetlic Empire) were not looking to make an open power play. Perhaps the snake-people wanted to maintain the strategic ambiguity of Utopia's status that was oh so conducive to smuggling or perhaps they simply weren't ready to commit the resources for conquest at the moment. Either way, it meant there was some room to manuever, even if that room wasn't that much larger than Sola's currently overcrowded office.

There was also the matter that Beesheelon had used her full name and the *Midama* honorific. That was a flex and maybe more than a bit of a threat. Beesheelon knew who Sola was, knew that she was from Silphus, a planet that had long had close and friendly ties with Invetilia prior to its imperial turn a few decades back. Silphus had been one of the first of the Invetlic Empire's conquests, back when Sola was a child. It was possible that Beesheelon simply meant they were more comfortable dealing with a Silphite than a typical Utopian. After all, hadn't Sola known how to greet her visitor and the meaning of that tongue gesture?

But this Inquirer wasn't born yesterday. She knew full well that Beesheelon was implying that they knew a great deal more about Sola's past. This might not be as threatening as the red-painted Bayhatile expected. Utopia was full of folks on the run from their past and looking for some kind of redemption, after all. Still, Sola didn't think a public disclosure would win her any friends. The room to maneuver was feeling quite cramped indeed.

So here Sola was, sitting in a nondescript office, negotiating Utopia's independence and continued existence with no real authority to back her up. On the bright side, no one else in Utopia had that authority either. And the caffeine was finally kicking in.

"You are confident that this fugitive came to Utopia?" The answer was obvious but Sola wanted to see if she could draw out more information before acting.

"Yes..." Beesheelon nodded. Not a Bayhatile expression but Beesheelon evidently wanted to be polite. *At least they're more diplomatic than the typical representative of the Invetlic Empire.* "We examined the Ring travel records. He definitely came here." *He? So likely not a Bayhatile then. Odd that they are going through this trouble for a member of one of the 'lesser' races.* "We believe he made a Switch here. Can we examine your records?"

Sola briefly considered lying and telling Beesheelon that Utopia's port didn't keep Switch records. For years now actually, she had been trying to convince the engineers up there to stop collecting such data for precisely this kind of reason. Unfortunately she had been unsuccessful thus far. All the engineers were descendants of several generations of Utopians and had grown more than a bit naïve to the dangers of excessive surveillance. Sola was more intimately familiar with the fact that no benevolent regime lasts forever and you never knew what the next boss would do with the tools you made. 

No, lying wasn't feasible here. Bayhatiles are rather adept at telling when a human was lying, certain more so than the other way around. And besides, Beesheelon would rather trivially be able to verify her statement anyways. Most of her fellow Utopians would no doubt want Sola to categorically refuse to give Beesheelon access to the Switch records, but then again, most of her fellow Utopians were a great deal more sanguine about their ability to fend off Invetlic incursions than Sola was.

The Inquirer reached for her tab. "Let me see if I can be of assistance. Can you supply me with the terrorist's name and the ID of the ship he came in on?"

A long silence met her questions and lingered. Most humans would have interpreted this as a refusal or even a rebuke. Bayhatiles had taught Sola many hard lessons over the years though, and chief among them was the importance of patience. Sola sipped her *mahtar* and mentally filed away the fact that Beesheelon was evidently reluctant to reveal the details of their mission.*Perhaps I'm not the only one operating in a tight space.*

"Aberseth Hamshell...he was a passenger on..." Sola's left eyebrow shot up as the Bayhatile continued to reel off details. 

*Aberseth Hamshell? What kind of name is that? Sounds human, but damn me if I have any clue about where from. Certainly not anywhere from within the Invetlic Empire.* Her fingers danced over the tab, running a query of Switch records. As rows of data appeared on the screen, Sola began to formulate a plan. 

"You are correct, this Hamshell did indeed come here." Beesheelon's large eyes blinked thrice in satisfaction. "He arrived five days ago — sorry, three by your time. He Switched without disembarking."

Beesheelon's head rose high in anticipation. "To where...?"

"Hob's Green." A small, verdant planet and a "protectorate" of the Invetlic Empire. Beesheelon would have no trouble throwing around their authority there. Another set of three blinks in satisfaction.

"I'm glad to be of help, *Seela* Beesheelon." This was a bald-faced lie and the Bayhatile knew it. But they had come here for information, not affection. 

"We thank you... for your swift... assistance, Inquirer Sola." Their tail vibrated for several seconds and then Beesheelon was turning to exit, impressively delicately for their size.

Sola had to repress a scoff as the Bayhatile made their way out of her office. She was quite familiar with the expression that Beesheelon had translated into Terran there. The correct translation wasn't "assistance." It was "compliance."

Alone again, Sola leaned forward on her desk, forehead resting on her knuckles. *I'm not wasn't out of the woods yet. None of us are.* She was assembling a mental to-do list, shuffling priorities and estimating how much time it would take to convince everyone who needed convincing. She had finally decided on her next course of action when a bald, tanned head poked its way around the doorframe.

"That was an Invetlic Bayhatile, wasn't it? And not from the hoi polloi either! Did you see that red spiral down by the twelfth vertebrae?" Meenshin let out a whistle. Meenshin was Sola's neighbor, a Root gardener and occasional assistant. He had been living in that same house next door for longer than Sola had been alive and, for all the old man's eccentricities, she frequently found his neighborhood familiarity invaluable in her line of work.

"Yes, Meen. I was the one who taught you about Bayhatile body symbols, remember?"

"That's riggghttt, you did, didn't you?" His eyes blinked rapidly, magnified by the thick-lensed glasses. Even on a backwater station like Utopia, such glasses were more of an archaic fashion statement than a medical necessity. Then again, Meenshin himself was a walking archaic fashion statement. 

"So what were they doing all the way out here? We gettin' invaded tomorrow?" This was said with all the concern of asking for the weather forecast.

Sola sighed. She rubbed her face, thinking about the row on her tab directly below the one she read off to Beesheelon. The one that said that, three days after Hamshell had Switched over towards Hob's Green, he had returned. And, as far as the port's records were concerned, he was still here. 

"Not if we can do something about it." She reached for a piece of paper and began scribbling down a note. "Meen, I need you to take this down to the hostel over on East Branch Rising."

"On the opposite side of Utopia! Not sure these old legs are up for that trip." Nonetheless, Meenshin entered the office more fully and approached the desk.

"You and I both know that you could walk the circumference of the station seven times over." Sola finished writing, folded the paper, and held it out to the old gardener.

"Find out if one Aberseth Hamshell is indeed staying there." Just before folded paper changed hands, Sola flicked her wrist and Meenshin closed his hands on empty air. "Not just checked in, I need to know if he is actually staying there. You give this note to him and him alone. Understand?"

Meenshin nodded. Sola offered the note again, this time genuinely. "And if he's not? Come back here?"

"No. We're going to walking all day, I'm afraid. And when we're not walking, we'll be arguing with every soul on this damn station." Sola was up and and grabbing her coat off the coat stand. It was her one piece of tailored clothing, made by one of the people on her list to visit today. It was a dark blue and came down almost to her ankles. A silver magnifying glass was embroidered over her heart. As much as it enveloped her, it was quite thin, as the climate-controlled station hardly warranted heavy layers of clothing. "My uniform" was what she called it. Utopia didn't really do uniforms, but Sola had found that sometimes a symbol of authority, even a home-made, half-assed one like this, could be helpful for someone in her calling. She suspected that today would be one of those times. "Message me with what you find, but keep it discreet. I don't want details in any recorded form. Then meet me..." Sola quickly estimated the time required for their respective errands.  "...over at the Diplomats place. If you beat me there, tell them we have an emergency and I will be there to discuss it shortly."

Meenshin nodded again, this time a bit dismissively. "This ain't an emergency. An emergency would be if the Invetlic's had a gunship parked up by the Crown." Sola shot him a stern glance and Meenshin waved one arm in surrender. "All right, all right, I'm going, I'm going. Don't hurry an old man."

As they stepped out the doorway and went their separate ways on the path, the elder towards the Crown and the younger further into the Roots, Meenshin's words followed the Inquirer. *Beesheelon didn't get here by walking. Maybe there **is** a gunship parked up by the Crown.*

***

As Sola walked the brisk walk of an Inquirer on a case, her pale blue eyes were drawn as always upwards towards the Tree. This a common tendency for those not born on Utopia. If you were born on a planet, or some other space station, or a moon, or a ship, or essentially anyplace that wasn't Utopia, it was difficult to believe that this place could exist, much less grow accustomed to it. Sola was going on twenty-five years on station — half her life — and she certainly wasn't use to it.

Utopia's basic layout was nominally that of a classic, rotating cylinder station design. The modifier "nominally" doing a great deal of work here, as cylindrical space stations date back to the beginning of virtually any species' first steps among the stars. An uncountable number of such stations, and their abandoned remnants, could be found scattered across the galaxy. And *none* of them looked *remotely* like Utopia. Utopia's ends were standard enough, two large metal disks with zero gravity ports at the center for ships to come and go via the usual Rings. Where the rounded outer wall of the cylinder would normally be, however, there was simply... nothing. Or rather, there *appeared* to be nothing. The two metal endcaps collectively contained a powerful force field generator, the kind normally seen on the massive military capital ships of major states. The resulting forcefield spanned the gap between the two discs, trapping an atmosphere inside and protecting the interior from the sparse debris and dust of interstellar space while leaving Utopia visually transparent to deep space. 

This meant that any approaching ship could see Utopia's dominating trait, the fact for which Utopia was known (besides its impressively stable anarchist system of government): a massive, genetically engineered Witonian *naraguna* tree, some two kilometers in height. The official name of the tree (to the extent that anything on Utopia could be considered "official") was "The Tree of Life," but it was virtually always referred to simply as "the Tree."

Surrounding the Tree were the inhabited portions of Utopia. A filigree of pathways and trusses and buildings and homes at varying distances from the center of the station, accommodating the various gravity preferences of the inhabitants. The roots split and branched, giving shape to dense neighborhoods on one end of the station. These roots entered to and exted from various buildings along their length. It was in these buildings that Root gardeners provided water and nutrients for the Tree, as well as the operating the greenhouse-esque gardens that fed Utopia. Other homes, workshops, and other buildings existed tucked in among the Roots, parallel to the Trunk, or among the countless leaves of the Crown ("the only crown that Utopia would ever acknowledge"). 

Utopia did not orbit a star. Instead it was positioned deep in interstellar space at a location convenient for Switching, enabling the station to serve as a minor trading port and for the inhabitants to import any goods that they couldn't produce for themselves (which was surprisingly little, given their limited material desires). Light was provided to the station by a series of massive light panels that rode on tracks around the circumference of the Trunk, traveling just slightly slower than the station as a whole rotated, resulting in a reasonably pleasant, 25 hour day-night cycle for the inhabitants of Utopia, assuming you spent that day along a single line of longitude. 

From an approaching ship, Utopia looked like a perfect, free floating bonsai tree encased in a cage of silvery spider webs. When standing on one of those pathways the Tree and the overall structure of Utopia simultaneously looked impossibly large and impossibly delicate to someone born on a genuine planet, such as Sola. Pairings of such contradictory impossibilities were what defined Utopia for Sola. For instance, the founders of the station did not intend to name it Utopia. For all their immense technical acumen (enough to entrust the lives of their community to a actively powered forcefield) and political idealism (to found an independent, anarchist station), they were not naïve. They didn't believe achieving utopia was possible. Rather, in keeping with their anarchist principles, they declined to name the station ("That which has no name is free of all limits"). But a sarcastic joke soon became commonplace among the inhabitants. At the completion of any project or upon achievement of any personal success, be it the construction of a new Root garden or the finishing of a pair of crocheted mittens, someone would ask, "So... are we done building utopia?" Eventually "utopia" became "Utopia" and the name of the station continued on, half aspirational, half self-deprecating. 

Sola however saw a different meaning in the name, another pairing of impossibilities. It is impossible to achieve utopia. They cannot not exist. A tree wrapped in web in deep space can't exist either. Nor can an anarchist community last through three hundred years and a dozen attempted occupations by neighboring governments. Utopia cannot exist. And yet it does.

*But for how much longer?* Sola pondered this as she made her way through the Roots towards the Dirt Port, simultaneously feeling like an ant climbing along a tree and an untethered EVA specialist who might trip and fall into the black at any moment.

***
Her first stop was about as quick — and about as irate — as she expected.

"YOU DID WHAT?!?" The person yelling at Sola was a lanky, young man. He was wearing one of the printed jumpers common in the port. He was clean shaven but with a long braid of dark hair. He was also one of the engineers of Dirt Port. Sola didn't have time to convince the entire community of engineers, so she had selected the one she knew the best. Jack was one of the more outgoing of the engineers and a fellow regular at at the Branched Path on rillball nights, which is how Sola had come to know him. They weren't exactly friends but they weren't strangers either, being fans of the same team and all. Her plan was to convince him and let him convince the rest of the engineers.

"You heard me." Sola's kept her voice low and calm. The pair were standing in the large open room full of workbenches. The benches and the floor — noticeably curved this close to the center of the station —  were covered in devices and components of various sizes, a mix of ongoing and abandoned projects. Most of the engineers were crouched over computer work stations at the moment, far enough away they couldn't overhear a conversation of lower volume but close enough that they couldn't help but overhear yelling.

"I know you're knew to Utopia but that's not how we do things here, we—" Sola resisted the urge to smack the engineer. *I have been living on this station for twenty-five years, you boy! You absolute child! That's almost as long as you've been alive!* The Inquirer remained focused on the task at hand, however. She let him prattle on for a bit before interrupting.

"...the principle of the thing. We have to maintain a certain level of solidar—"

"Jack, you need to spend a lot less time adjudicating what already happened and lot more time thinking about what's going to happen next." This caught the engineer by surprise. He squinted his eyes at Sola, hurrying to reorient his racing thoughts.

"And what's that?"

"I have reason to believe that the Invetlics are going to be back in a few days. And they're going to ask for another query of the Switch and Ring records. A much more exhaustive query this time." Jack was well and truly befuddled now. Sola gave him the space to think it through.

"Oh...Oh! You sent them on a wild goose chase!" Jack laughed, a surprisingly melodic sound. "You lied to them about where the revolutionary went! And when they come back, you can send them off in another direction!"

"It's bad for your health to lie to a Bayhatile, Jack. I didn't lie. And I won't lie when they return." The mirth dropped off of Jack's face immediately.

"Then wha— Nevermind. If you aren't going to lie, tell them to pound bark then. The entire station will back you up, I'm sure."

Sola shook her head resolutely. "When they come back, I will conduct the query they ask of me and I will tell them what I find."

"Well, then we'll take away your access to the database!" 

*Now he's finally on the right track,* Sola thought to herself.

"That's a good first step. But they won't stop when they can't get answers from me. What's your security like around here? What are you going to do when that snake or their goons come slithering around here."

Jack's eyes widened and he glanced around to see if anyone had overheard. "Sola! You can't say thinks like that! You can't call a Bayhatile a —"

"Jack," the Inquirer leaned in close to the engineer, "Stop telling me what to do and get back to thinking about what *you're* going to do." She said this softly but sternly, clearly enunciating each word and maintaining unblinking eye contact throughout.

The engineer leaned back away from Sola's intimidating stare. He crossed his arms and stared at the ceiling, as if he could find the answers written then.  He mumbled under his breath, clearly lost in thought. Sola waited for him to reach a conclusion and return to station. When he finally did so, it was with a dramatic sigh.

"Fine. Fine! You win. We'll purge the records and then we'll work out some way to anonymously collect just what we need for logistics planning and operational forecasting moving forward."

Sola returned to upright. She grinned slightly and extended a hand. "We thank you for your swift assistance, Engineer Jack."

Jack squinted his eyes a bit, a mix of mild confusion and disgust, reluctant to accept the offered hand. Then, unable to figure out Sola's joke, shrugged and turned towards the other engineers, who were all doing their best to listen in without looking like they were listening in and largely failing at both.

"'I need a few volunteers to work on a priority project! We gotta purge the transpo records and we gotta do it now!" This announcement was met with exclamations of surprise and anger. Several people stood and began walking over to Jack in the languid, loping gait that was necessary in the lower gravity sections of Utopia like this.

Sola reached out and put a friendly hand on Jack's shoulder. "So. Will I see you this weekend? The West Branch Woodpeckers have a new starter." This case was important, but she wanted to avoid burning any bridges if she could.

Jack looked back over his shoulder at her with more than a little exasperation. "Of course! It's about time the Mid-Trunk Revolutionaries had a good ass-kicking. Now don't you someone else to bother?"

Sola chuckled and nodded, then turned to go, as Jack struck up a heated discussion with his fellow engineers. Most of it was about technical details of little interest to her, but on her way out, she did catch one snippet.

"— that damn Inquisitor can't —"

"Etein, you can't call her that!" This last was Jack.

Sola pretended that she hadn't heard anything. 

Perhaps that overheard insult bothered her more than she realized, as she was less aware of her surroundings then she should of been while exiting the bustling Port area. A man turned a corner and slammed into her hard. Sola managed to roll him off her side as she side-stepped, thereby only just keeping from going down on the ground, despite the bulk and speed of the clumsy oaf. He, meanwhile, stumbled heavily, bent far forward and windmilling wildly to keep from going over. This caused baggy layers of patchwork grays, greens, and blacks — the non-uniform hallmark of a smuggler —  to rise and fall about him in great voluminous waves of cloth, accentuated in the low gravity environment. 

"Watch where you're going, bitch!" The man shouted as he finally recovered his balance, not even deigning to look over his shoulder at the person he collided into. His invective was raspy in the extreme, more a loud whisper than a speaking voice, indicative of some disease, injury, or excessive drug use.

Sola responded by thrusting a closed fist at the rude smuggler and slapping her bicep twice with her other hand, an obscene Silphite gesture of extreme disdain. 

Evidently the man heard the slaps and recognized them for what they were, as he raised a middle finger over his shoulder — a gesture much more universal among the human diaspora — though he still didn't look back.

Rarely one to back down from a fight, Sola briefly considered escalating, but decided her present case took priority. Besides, it was unbecoming for a Utopian to resort to violence over so trivially a matter. She settled for muttering some further profanities under her breath and turned to depart herself.

***

Her next stop brought her about halfway back to her office, in the core of the South Roots neighborhood, also known as Tanglewood for the sheer density of Tree roots. This resulted in the paths and bulidings taking on confusing serpentine paths that wound around, over, and under the many roots, looking for all the world like some kind of metallic, negative-image symbiot of the roots. The density of the neighborhood, as well as its positioning far from the Trunk, meant that little daylight reached here and the neighborhood was instead dependent on more conventional street lights. It also meant that the black and glitter of space was hidden from view more so here than almost any neighborhood of Utopia. To walk around here was to feel like you were actually inside an enclosed station for once. A surprisingly arboreal and hilly station, but a station nonetheless.

Eventually her winding path took her to Madam Métier's Tailoring and Fiber Arts Workshop. At that moment, her tab vibrated. Sola checked it and there was a message from Meenshin.

"Checked in. Hasn't been here since."

The Inquirer nodded at this confirmation of her suspicions. The tab went back in the coat pocket and Sola stepped up to the door. A small posse of youths from a couple different species were leaning up against the wall only a few feet off. Sola gave an informal two-fingered salute to Jeanine, the only one she recognized. Jeanine returned the gesture. With that done, Sola pushed her way inside the workshop, causing a small bell to jingle as she entered.

"Welcome, neighbor!" A voice called from deeper in the space, past shelves and tables full of mounds of rolled cloth and thread. "I'm back here if you need help with anything."

Sola navigated the narrow, colorful pathways leading to the back. Turning yet another corner, she found her quarry. The proprietress of the establishment was helping a client select a fabric for some project or another.

"This would go oh so well with your eyes, but if you think it too bright, this one would be a beautiful alternative." The tailor looked as lovely as always. She was wearing an indigo dress patterned with dark green diamonds, no doubt made with her own hands. It was sleeveless and came down to her knees, thereby showing off both her graceful arms and her sculpted calves. Her dirty blonde hair was tied up bun using a ribbon in such a manner that it was impossible to tell how long it had taken her to put it up, as it looked simultaneously effortless and immaculate. 

Sola tried to discretely wait a polite distance away, but the tailor looked over her shoulder to identify the entrant. Sola met those grey eyes that could dance so easily between good cheer and scorn, the eyes of Madam Métier.

Métier was the best tailor on Utopia, not that she had much competition. Most people onboard the station wore clothes fabricated by the printers. They never fit quite right and they degraded faster than ideal, but they were quick to produce and easily recycled back into feedstock for the printers. Clothes of cloth, much less tailored clothes, were thus a luxury. Material luxuries were not prohibited in Utopia, but they were frowned upon. An exception was made for the crafts, however.  Métier actually spent very little of her time making or adjusting clothes. Instead she taught those interested in learning how to sew, knit, crochet, or perform virtually any of the fiber arts. Her workshop was thus less a store than a community classroom.

"My dear, dear, Inquisitor! What causes you to darken my doorstep during daylight hours? You better not have damaged that lovely coat I made you!" Métier quickly closed the distance between the two and pulled the Inquirer into an embrace. Sola was used to the tailor's effusive displays of affection and thus only tensed *moderately* at the touch.

The Inquirer role was not a popular one on Utopia. Historically, it was largely performed by bright-eyed and bushy-tailed newcomers to the station who got voluntold to do it. They usually lasted a few months, maybe even a year or two before realizing, "Hey, this is an anarchist society. I don't have to do this if I don't want to." Then someone else would be found. Sola, with her twenty two years, had been serving for perhaps longer than any Inquirer in Utopian history (due to the high turnover, Inquirer records weren't exactly great, so confirming this would be difficult). This tenure hadn't won her any more friends though. A third of that station called her Inquisitor behind her back. Another third did so to her face. Métier was the only one who did so with affection.

"I need to speak to you privately," Sola murmured into her ear.

Métier took a step back, though she kept her hands on Sola's arms. She looked appraisingly at the Inquirer briefly before nodding decisively. She spun about, causing the hem of her dress to flare slightly.

"I'm afraid that I need to step away for a moment, neighbor, but the sewing machines are available for your use once you pick out a fabric. If you run into any trouble, just pause and I will help you work through it later."

The tailor began walking away, as she did so she raised her left arm and flicked her wrist in a beckoning gesture. "Come, come, my dear. We can speak in the fitting room."

A few moments later, the pair were in the cramped fitting room. Métier immediately (and rather elegantly) occupied the one bench in the room, so Sola was forced to stand on the raised platform, surrounded by mirrors. 

"So. Trouble?"

"I'm afraid so." Sola quickly relayed the events in her office that morning.

You don't go through something like that with another person without coming out with some form of either friendship or hatred. Tailoring was Métier's hobby, not her vocation. Her real job was professional revolutionary. This is what had driven her from Silphus those many years ago. That flight to Utopia had hardly meant she was ready to go into retirement however. If anything, her career had kicked into overdrive. Before long, she had become the hub of revolutionary communications, materiel support, and general solidarity across a dozen polities. That possé outside her door were some combination of colleagues, students, and bodyguards. While Utopia was an awfully peaceful place, you never knew when a member of one secret police or another might try offing the Madam of Revolution. Not that she wasn't capable of defending herself. For all of Métier's outward display of grace and delicacy, she was far from the Utopian pacifist norm and could street brawl with the best of them. Sola had seen that first hand on more than one occasion.

Utopia valued Métier's political work much more highly than her skill with a needle and thread. While few Utopians were politically evangelical, they commonly felt some moral obligation to support those who had not yet achieved liberty for themselves. They were thus happy to harbor Métier and her many colleagues.

This did however introduce a certain tension into Métier and Sola's relationship. Sola's job was to root out secrets while Métier's was to keep them hidden. And for Métier's part, she had a certain ideological skepticism regarding Sola's career choice. Her use of the "Inquisitor" appellation was only partly in jest. On the other hand, their friendship could occasionally benefit their professional roles. More than once they had worked together to root out spies and saboteurs who had infiltrated Utopia. And if a revolutionary ever stirred up enough trouble to land on Sola's desk (and young revolutionaries were often stirring up such trouble), the Inquirer could bring it Métier and trust her to take care of her own. Still, the pair had to be careful to not step on each other's toes. The primary expression of their friendship was purely casual: regular nights at the Branching Path where they watched rillball (Sola's choice), played Silphite card game lumil (Métier's choice), and drank (both of their choice). 

"Sorry, sorry, I misspoke." Sola's hands went up in defense and she backed up until she clumsily bumped into the mirrors behind her. "I meant to say that I came to pass on a warning to you. *If* you know how to reach Hamshell, I ask that you pass it on. I actively *don't* want to know anything more about the man."

Métier seemed placated by this apology. The fire in her eyes vanished as swiftly as it had arisen. "Very well then. I forgive you, my dear. Is there anything other than what you already told me?"

Sola pondered this for a moment. "Well, I expect that Beesheelon will be back in a few days. The folks over at Hob's Green won't try to protect a Invetilic fugitive from an agent of the empire. I convinced the port engineers to clear their records, but —"

"Oh good job, Sol!" Métier eyes' sparkled with amusement and joy. Like Sola, she was all too familiar with the potential oppressive uses of excessive surveillance and had made her own (unsuccessful) attempt to persuade the engineers to reduce their data collection.

The Inquirer smiled faintly at the praise. "But, I don't think that will slow down the Invetlic's for long. They want this guy, Mé. They want him bad. He's got to be prepared." ***You've** got to be prepared*, she thought to herself.

Métier nodded thoughtfully. "I suppose your next stop is the Diplomats?"

Sola returned the nod. "Meenshin will be waiting there for me."

Métier flashed a brilliant smile. "Well then, why don't I accompany you? We'll get that lovely walk in the Crown after all!" She leapt to her feet, smoothed her dress, and took Sola's elbow in her own, before practically dragging the Inquirer out of the workshop.

A few mornings later, Sola was once again at her desk, sipping *mahtar* and flipping through some case notes. This time, she was nursing a massive hangover. The Mid-Trunk Revolutionaries had indeed finally had their asses kicked by the new Woodpeckers starter, something deserving of more than a little celebration. Sola and Jack had even drunkenly embraced amid the rowdy hubbub that ensued. Not even Sola getting her own ass thoroughly kicked by Métier at cards had dampened the mood. Now, though, she was suffering the consequences.

Her most recent case was about a theft. Or rather "a material discrepancy" in Utopian parlance. A rather sizable amount of printer feedstock had disappeared from one of the larger and better organized workshops. What's more is that it looked like someone had been altering the inventory records to cover up their pilfering. So now Sola was scrolling through those records, seeing if she could pinpoint when this mess got started. Even on a place like Utopia, which lacked any laws to speak of, societal pressure could make some folks turn to the surreptitious. 

It was possible that the stuff had been smuggled off-station, sold in exchange for some personal luxuries. The Inquirer didn't think so though. As valuable as feedstock was on Utopia, it wasn't worth all that much elsewhere. Not the kind of thing that someone would go through the risk of sneaking out. No, Sola was confident that there was an extremely dumb, ill-conceived, and wasteful art project at the bottom of this case. She might be better off pulling on that thread than sifting through spreadsheets. 

Sola kept finding herself distracted by the Hamshell case though. Not Hamshell himself, she didn't give a rat's ass about him. She was thinking of the broader political ramifications. Would the plans and contingencies she had cooked up with the Diplomats and everyone else work? Sola was far from sure. *Nothing for it. You gotta play to your outs.* She reached for her mug once more.

At this moment, a large green reptilian form slithered in through her open door. One virtue of Sola's object of distraction is that she wasn't surprised this time. Instead she calmly rose and rotated her head politely.

"Greetings, Beesheelon. How can I be of assistance?" Sola chose to remained standing. She wasn't sure how the Bayhatile was going to take the coming news and she wanted to be ready to move quickly if need be. Thankfully she was already wearing her Inquirer coat to try and cope with her body's hangover-induced inability to thermoregulate. 

"We... followed the fugitive to... Hob's Green." Beesheelon seemed unperturbed at Sola's posture. Perhaps they simply didn't notice the oddity of a human doing so. They certainly seemed a bit more frazzled than their last visit. As if they were operating under some kind of time constraint. "We... investigated... his actions there."

Sola remained silent. She hadn't been asked anything yet and so no reason to reveal information unnecessarily.

"We found... that shortly after arriving... he returned here."

"To Utopia?" Sola's eyes widened in what she hoped would pass for genuine surprise.

"Indeed... " No polite nod this time. "We would like to request another examination of your Switch records."

*Well, here's the moment of truth.* Sola put on her best "polite refusal" expression. "I'm afraid I cannot assist you this time."

Beesheelon blinked once. Slowly. A clear expression of displeasure. Before the Bayhatile could speak up, however, Sola pressed on. "You see, the Port Engineers, who maintain those records, learned that I ran a search on behalf of a foreign criminal investigation. They were... displeased."

Those large eyes studied the Inquirer's face carefully. "We... see. They... revoked your access to the records?"

Sola could practically see the gears turning in the mind of this representative of the Invetilic Empire. Already their attention was turning to whoever did have access and what pressures could be brought to bear on them.

"Not exactly, *Seela* Beesheelon," Sola hoped that she wasn't laying it on too thick with this expression of polite deference. "Rather, they elected to delete the records entirely and stop collecting such data moving forward."

The Bayhatile stared silently for a long while, unblinking for far longer than a human could. They were evidently astonished. As a well-placed member of a highly organized, centralized state, they were having trouble with the idea that someone they thought of as a customs official just outright ceasing data collection. Cook the books, sure, but purge them altogether? How was the Port to function? How was the Utopian government to function? Customs were a *core* activity of any polity!

*Welcome to Utopia, neighbor,* Sola snidely thought to herself, though she took care to keep any trace of a smile from showing on her face. Eventually the silence stretched longer than what constituted polite even for a Bayhatile, so the Inquirer stepped in.

"Well, if he came from Hob's Green and didn't head back your way, there's only three or four other places he could have gone from here." Sola chose not to bring up the possibility that Hamshell was still present on Utopia.

"Yes..." Beesheelon was still clearly distracted by the Port records revelation. "We shall... return to our ship... and consider... our next action." 

Sola barely restrained a sigh of relief. "Very well, if I can assist you in any other way, feel welcome to return." But the Bayhatile was already gone, the door closed behind them, clearly in a hurry.

*Somewhat concerning. They seem pressured. Frantic even. Frantic people act rashly.* Sola slowly sat back down. *Nothing for it now. The ball's in their court. And we're ready for the return.* At least this wasn't her problem anymore. If the Beesheelon came back with Invetlic firepower, it wouldn't be Sola's responsibility to do anything about it. The Volunteer Diplomats, the Port Engineers, and several other groups would handle it. No, Sola had done her job — and done it pretty cleanly in her own estimation. Let others deal with the mess for awhile.

The Inquirer let out a deep sigh and let her eyes return to the documents about the printer feedstock case. She reached for her *mahtar*. As the mug touched her lips, her office door was flung open. A man rushed in and slammed both of his palms on her desk, causing her to startle and spill her drink on her shirt. He was a stranger to her. An adult, but young. Maybe early thirties. Short. Somewhat rotund. With closely cropped brown hair and dark skin.

"My name is Aberseth Hamshell! You gotta help me!"

*Fuck me in the ass with the Tree!*

"What..." *gasp* "just..." *wheezed* "happened?" Aberseth was bent over, desperately gulping in ragged breaths. 

It was a fair question. One moment he was standing in Sola's office, demanding her assistance, and the next he was down three flights of stairs towards the perimeter of the station, along several straight corridors, and around quite a few turns heading towards the Roots of Tanglewood.  One of those Roots was curled around the pair now, granting them a measure of privacy in the outdoors setting.

So she had moved him — rather swiftly — out her back door and along one of numerous escape routes she had memorized. She may have been living on Utopia for more than two decades but that didn't mean she had let her guard down. Sola was stronger than she looked and she was practiced at moving the semi-to-un-cooperative. Aberseth's winded state was more a function how he had arrived at her office than the flight from it. Sola didn't think his feet had even touched the ground more than a couple of times.

The Inquirer was breathing heavily herself, but still upright, her eyes scanning the approaches to their temporary resting point. "Invetelic was in my office not five minutes before you showed." A few deep breaths. "Might have seen you on your way in."

Aberseth blanched at this. A moment later, some combination of fear hormones transformed his gasps into dry retching. 

*If he's faking, he's a damn fine actor.* Sola was quite familiar with what genuine terror looked like and say no reason to doubt this man. She waited patiently and took the opportunity to order her thoughts. Those thoughts essentially being, *Why the fuck is everyone making this fucker my problem?* 

Finally, Aberseth recovered some measure of his composure, though he sank down into a sitting position.

"What did you want my help with?" She kept her tone and expression polite and professional despite her internal screaming.

"I went out for a walk around South Branch Falling. First time leaving my room since I arrived. Needed fresh air." Aberseth was rubbing his eyes with both palms, as if trying to wake up from a nightmare. "On my way back, my remote alarm went off. Someone was in my room. They much have found me. I need protection."

There were a lot of jumps in logic there, but Sola could revisit those later if needed. 

"I'm not a bodyguard. Utopians don't even carry weapons." 

This was true in the general sense. While the Port had its security measures, Utopians strongly discouraged possession, much less use, of weapons. In the case of one Inquirer Sola, however, this was patently false. She had three weapons on her person at this very moment in fact: brass knuckles, a telescoping baton, and a handgun. The brass knuckles she had occasion to use thrice, all against sentients smugglers who thought that "anarchist space station" meant that their kind would be welcome on Utopia. The baton she had only used once, against her own client in a truly bizarre case. The gun she had never even pulled, something she was thankful for, as even the baton use had almost lost her the position of Inquirer. It was only her client loudly and repeatedly telling everyone who would listen that the whole incident had, in fact, been his idea that had spared her.

"Why don't you reach out to the folks who hid you in that room in the first place?" 'The folks,' of course, would be Métier's crew, but Sola wasn't about to give Aberseth any name that he didn't already have.

"The tailor's not answering my calls. No one is at her shop. And she said that if I was ever in trouble and couldn't reach her, I should go Inquirer Sola and she would protect me with her life."

*Fuck off, Métier! Really?* Protecting a client with one's life was decidedly *not* part of the Inquirer job description nor was it part of Sola'spersonal code. She should haul him back onto his feet and drag him to the Volunteer Diplomats. Let them deal with this mess and let her get back to solving petty thefts.

"Getting someone to track down the tailor."

"What if they're the mole?!?" Sola stopped what she was doing and looked at Aberseth. Wild terror shone in his eyes.

"Aberseth." Sola interrupted kindly but firmly. She knew the terror that Aberseth was feeling all to well, much to her shame. "Listen to me. If you trust everyone, you'll end up with a knife in the gut. But unless you want to find a hole to curl up in and die, you gotta trust someone." *And then you desperately hope that they don't stab you in the back.* "You trust the tailor. The tailor trusts me. I trust Meenshin."

"You're right. I'm dead on my own and don't have anyone else but you at the moment." 

*So he can be reasonable.*

"That's right, friend. You gotta play to your outs and right now, that's me." Sola extended a hand. Aberseth took it, expecting a handshake agreement, but found himself being hauled to his feet.

"Let's get a move on then." The Inquirer began walking, clearly expecting her client to follow.

Where are we going? Some safehouse?" He stumbled a bit as he got up to speed. "

Sola chuckled. "No. We're going to that room of yours."

"Wait, what? Are you crazy? They're probably waiting for us there!"

Sola shook her head. "I doubt it. Whoever this was broke into you room as soon as you left it. That's not luck. They were waiting for you to leave. Which means they wanted something in your room, not you."

She could see the gears turning in Aberseth's brain. He was bright in some kind of way, Sola could tell, but he knew little about the covert life. Intriguing.

"So what kind of revolutionary are you?" 

"Oh I'm not a revolutionary." Aberseth said this quickly and defensively. "I'm not political at all. I don't pay attention to that kind of thing."

The Inquirer raised an eyebrow slightly but didn't say anything. She could be brusque and blunt to the point of rudeness when solving a problem, such as with Engineer Jack at the Port or when relocating Aberseth out of her office, but Sola was now in detective mode. Sure enough, Aberseth soon began revising his statement. "Well...I mean, I wasn't political until recently, I suppose. It was kind of forced on me."

Sola nodded empathetically. People often like to think of revolutionaries as committed ideologues, driven by a principled belief in liberation. And some were. But many were simply pushed into by their oppressor. A lost job, an arrested family member, some casual act of brutality by a cop. 

"I'm a scientist. A virologist. I was working for the Invetelic Empire. I found out they were planning on re-purposing my research. I... I couldn't let that happen."

Sola stopped in her tracks and turned to face her client. She re-evaluated him from top to bottom. The man before her wasn't a young rebel who printed the wrong pamphlet or told the wrong joke in public. He was something altogether more dangerous. A naïve fool with a hand grenade. No wonder Beeshelon seemed so frantic.

Aberseth waited for several long seconds, apparently expecting for more from the Inquirer. Eventually he realized that that was all he was getting and his eyebrows shot up. One hand exited his pocket and he scratched his forehead, just under the hairline.

"Oh, well, okay. Let me figure out how to explain this then. I was... no, no. How about... Let's start..."

"Viral bioweapons have many limitations. They tend to spread uncontrollably, even when targeted at a narrow genetic profile. And infectious diseases, even novel ones, tend to be caught by the automated medical exams at any half-way decent customs process. Once identified, vaccine development is trivial with modern methods. I was working on an algorithm, a method, a framework of sorts, that could be used to develop a viral bioweapon that bypasses many of these limitations.

"Imagine a virus that is highly infectious and spreads through the air. It primarily lives somewhere small, out of the way, in low concentrations, like your pineal gland. Or your appendix. Importantly, it has virtually no symptoms. All this means that virtually no screening would catch it."

Sola crossed her arms. "What's the point of a bioweapon with no symptoms?"

"Exactly! The virus is designed to be innocuous only for a certain number of generations from its initial release. Once it reaches that age, it begins causing infected cells to generate a small amount of a dangerous prion that in turn replicate and begin shutting down the host body in a matter of hours. A day or two at most. All of this happens near simultaneously in all infected hosts. No time to identify the virus, much less develop a vaccine. And even with a vaccine, prion diseases are notoriously difficult to treat. An entire ship, station, even a planet, gone just like that." Aberseth snapped his fingers.

Sola didn't know what a prion was, but she didn't think that was particularly important. She got the gist. "What about the control problem?"

"Ah yes, there are three answers to that. The first is the classic genetic targeting. Find some genetic trait relatively unique to the target population and have either the virus or the prion only function in that population. The second is the near simultaneous fatalities. If everyone with the virus dies quickly, the virus cannot spread further. The third is a kill timer. The virus starts producing prions after a certain number of generations, but a certain number of generations after that, it dissolves itself." Aberseth, now with both hands out of his pockets, put both fists together and then opened them in a pantomime of disintegration. His voice held a strange mix of horror and pride.

Some measure of disgust must have found its way onto Sola's face, because Aberseth began waving his hands about while shaking his head.

"Don't get me wrong, I'm not weapons developer! I was doing more fundamental research, real science. This use case didn't even cross my mind! I only found out what the Invetlics planned to do with it but chance. And as so as I knew, I made plans to escape. I downloaded all my notes and code and lab results, then wiped the lab computers, the official backups, the unofficial backups that my assistant maintained, and even the secret one that the Invetelic Security Service thought I didn't know about." Aberseth was smiling now, clearly pleased at his cleverness. Sola had to admit she was somewhat impressed, though it was of the patronizing variety usually reserved for circus animals.

"You took the data with you? Why not just delete it?"

It was Aberseth's turn to show shocked disgust. "Delete my life's work?!? Of course not! Besides, I need proof to show everyone what the Invetelics are planning." This second reason was said after a delay, with much less emphasis than the first, a clear *post hoc* justification for his actions. 

A cold dread began stirring in Sola'sbelly as several pieces began clicking into place. "Were those files in your room? Or did you keep them with you?"

Aberseth's eyes dropped to avoid the furious accusation in the Inquirer's stare. "I left them in my room."

Sola'slips narrowed in restrained fury. "Aberseth Hamshell, for the love of all that is holy, tell me that those files are encrypted."

Aberseth raised his face and tilted his head in surprise. "Of course! State of the art encryption. I'm not an idiot!" Sola' slight frame relaxed an almost imperceptible degree. "It requires both a password and a hardware token to unlock. The password I have memorized. The hardware token I gave to the tailor, so that even if the Invetelics caught up to me, they couldn't just beat the password out of me." 

Sola had to admit (silently to herself of course, no need to stroke his ego), this was pretty reasonable. It did, however cast Métier's absence in altogether more sinister light. The dread in Sola's chest had been subsiding. Now it came roaring back and climbed up from her belly into her heart. The Inquirer extended a hand to a nearby wall and leaned on it, eyes closed, trying to steady herself. Aberseth shifted his weight nervously, unsure how to respond to this expression of frailty in his protector. 

*Nothing for it at the moment,* Sola told herself. *Meenshin is handling that side of thing and Métier's crew will already be on it. You need to follow the lead available to you.*

Her eyes shot open, startling the scientist, who took a step back. Sola rose back to her full height and resumed walking, faster this time.

"Come on. We have a crime scene to search."

Sola ultimately decided against taking Aberseth back to his room. While the research materials were clearly the primary target, rather than the scientist, whoever this thief was may have had time to realize that they were encrypted. If so, they would no doubt come back. Instead, Sola decided dropped off Aberseth with her friend Mino, one of the station's apothecaries. A big furry bear of a man, Mino produced pharmaceuticals, industrial chemicals, and recreational drugs for the residents of Utopia. He was well situated to be the temporary guardian for Sola's fugitive ward. Foreigners sometimes got a bit too handsy with apothecary wares, so labs like Mino's tended to have better security than most of the station. The lab was positioned in the core of the Roots, along Gnarled Way, decently close to the trunk, as lower gravity tended to be helpful for some kinds of crystal-related chemical reactions, as Mino had told Sola on more than one occasion. 

On the way there, aware of Aberseth's anxiety and nervousness, Sola tried some small talk in an effort to distract him. Of course, for her, small talk essentially meant less directed questioning.

"So how did you end up working for the Invetelics? A name like yours, doesn't sound like your from anywhere in the Argentum Arm."

Aberseth's nervousness was instantly replaced with a tense resentment. A cold, steely look flared up in his dark eyes. 

"I'm from Mars." The young scientist's entire posture was daring her — *just daring her* — to offer some trite condolences. 

The Inquirer instead let out a vague grunt instead. Utopia was a long way from her namesake system, but *that* story had traveled much further still. And while Sola could certainly empathize with being forced to flee one's homeworld under adverse conditions, she recognized that she had no real understanding of what Aberseth had been through. *And he must have been only a child too.*

Having completely failed the task of mild small talk, the pair walked the rest of the way in silence.

Thankfully, Mino's lab managed to turn Aberseth's mood around. It turned out that Aberseth found the low budget, DIY lab (one of Utopia's finest) to be some mix of quaint and ingenious, in a pragmatic sort of way. He had immediately launched into technical questions and suggestions that went right over Sola's head. Mino seemed to bear it patiently however, and was perhaps even enjoying himself a bit. They had hardly noticed when Sola slipped out to continue her investigatory work. The sound of their chattering voices as she departed took a load off of the Inquirer's shoulders. Aberseth would be in good hands there.

Sola breathed deeply upon exiting Mino's lab. While one of the perks of her vocation was getting to visit all the nooks and crannies of Utopia, she wasn't much a fan of the abundant chemical and biological labs on the station. She understood that, lacking the natural resources of a planet, Utopians had to be more resourceful in synthesizing and recycling all that they could, but the smell! 

Arriving at Aberseth's lodging, Sola first checked in with the proprietor. Turns out that Jamus had been out gathering kitchen ingredients this morning and, having left their doors unlocked in typical Utopian fashion, was thus was unaware of any intrusion. They gladly showed the Inquirer to the room in question and left her to investigate.

The room was less lodging place and more converted storage site. Windowless, with a single light built in flush with the ceiling, and brushed metal walls and floor, it was hardly a hospitable place to lay your head. Sola could see why Aberseth felt the need to go on a walk after a few days in here. The furniture in the room was a bit better. A standard Utopian single bed, desk, and some storage units, all printed out of plasticon and patterned with something vaguely wood-like. Nothing fancy but far from trashy, though Sola suspected that someone of Aberseth's background would find it more than a bit spartan. Then again, he *was* a scientist. Sometimes they didn't care much for fineries or unnecessary comforts.   

The room was a mess and very clearly had been searched. There were a variety of ways to search a room though and Sola was a bit of a connoisseur. This was not the wild trashing that some organized crime thugs or totalitarian secret police would conduct as a means of intimidation. Drawers were pulled out, but the contents were all emptied onto the bed rather than the floor. The one chair was upside down, but not broken. This was also not the frantic searching of the desperate or amateur. Whoever had been here had clearly conducted a thorough search starting on the right and working counterclockwise through the storage units before reaching the desk, where the search stopped before reaching the bed on her left. This was the work of a professional with a clear objective in mind.

Sola swiftly stepped into the room and knelt down, looking into the vacant space left by the removed third desk drawer from the top (now sitting on top of the desk with its brethren). This was where Aberseth had told her the drive had been hidden. Sure enough, it was gone. *Too much to hope for, I suppose.*

Sola stepped back into the doorway so the entire room was in view once more. She drew her biological penlight out of her pocket and began scanning the room with it, looking for some fingerprint or piece of hair where it shouldn't be, anything that could lead her back to who had done this. 

As she looked over Aberseth's clothes strewn on the bed, something snagged her attention. A faint chemical smell. The flutter of dark cloth. More a memory than a present sensory experience. Then it was gone.

Immediately Sola halted her search and stood still. She emptied her mind and began swepping over the scene again with, this time with soft eyes. She wasn't looking for the data drive, assessing the scene, or searching for anything in particular. She was simply allowing the room and its contents to present themselves to her on their own terms. Hopefully whatever had triggered the momentary sensation would trigger it again. Something as tenuous as this could not be forced, it would come or not of its own accord. After many long minutes, Sola was on the verge of admitting defeat. That connection her brain had so briefly made remained dormant. It would not rouse again from wherever it slept in the recesses of her mind. Not now at least.

But then she saw something else. Something out of place in the scene. An accident, though a small one. On the back-right corner of desk was a small, unpainted ceramic figurine of an androgynous human sitting cross-legged. Its right hand was placed upon its heart. It's left arm was extended, open palm up. It was almost the same brown as the desk itself. This would have been placed here by Jamus, not Aberseth, as it was the classic Utopian spirit of hospitality, a tradition of uncertain origin dating more than a century back. Its presence was unremarkable. The chip in its knee was. Damage to such a figurine was considered highly unlucky and Jamus would have been sure to replace it forthwith, had they known.

The Inquirer ducked under the desk and looked in the corner with her penlight. Sure enough, there was the missing fragment of the figurine. And a droplet of blood, part on the floor, part on the fragment. A grim smile spread on Sola's face and she let out a silent whoop of celebration in her mind. She pulled out a small, seal-able bag and careful scooped the fragment up. Upon extricating herself from the underside of the desk, she looked over the scene to sort out the sequence of events. How had this come to pass?

The intruder was searching for the data drive and had reached the desk. Perhaps after searching the underside, they had removed the drawers from the desk, one by one, searching it thoroughly before placing it on the desk from left to right. When placing the third and final drawer, they had failed to notice the figurine and accidentally pushed it off the edge of the desk into the empty space only a few centimeters wide between the desk and the wall. Having been working swiftly, carefully, and quietly to avoid the attention of Jamus or the return of Aberseth, this consequence of clumsiness had caught them offguard. They had lunged after the tumbling ceramic, perhaps out of some futile attempt to keep the figurine from clattering on the floor, perhaps simply out of instinct. 

As they hastily reached forward, some piece of exposed skin between their glove and sleeve had scrapped the edge of the drawer sitting on the table. Not enough to meaningfully injure, maybe not even enough to be noticed, but just enough to break the skin. Failing to prevent the figurine from tumbling to the floor, the intruder had gone under the desk to retrieve it. Why was that? Perhaps because they wanted to confirm exactly what it was they had knocked over. Perhaps because they were superstitious about disturbing the hospitality spirit. Either way, reaching back into the corner, they had grabbed the figurine, leaving behind a single drop of blood as they did so. Having satisfied themselves that the object was nothing of import, they returned it to its rightful place, not noticing their minor cut or the missing fragment. Finally they had resumed their search for the data drive and no doubt found quite soon thereafter.

Sola didn't let herself get carried away by this unexpected success, however. She resumed her meticulous search, seeing if she could turn up any more leads. Unfortunately there was nothing more of any worth and that latent memory remained so. Not that she could complain. She had found more here than she could have hoped for.

***

Sola had two choices for testing the blood she had found. The first was the (extremely loosely organized) Medical Cooperative, who handled the bulk of the station's healthcare needs. If this intruder was a station resident or had ever received healthcare here, the Cooperative would likely be able to identify them from the blood sample. There were two problems here, however. The first was the fact that Sola thought it unlikely that the intruder was a Utopian. This was a professional operative, likely brought in to deal with this very new and very particular situation. Even if he was in the Cooperative's records by some miracle, the physicians were famously protective of patient privacy. Sola had convinced them to share information before, but that had been a straightforward murder case (a quite rare occasion in her career on Utopia). While the Inquirer certainly felt like the present case rose to a similar level of severity, it was also complicated. Persuading the right people might take quite some time.

The other option were the Port Quarantine Lab. As was common on virtually every space station across the Argentum, Utopia screened every person and item that came through for pathogens and other dangerous organisms. This involved a small blood draw (for species that had blood). As Sola was keenly aware, the Port was much more free with its records than the Medical Cooperative. Of course, she had also recently convinced them to delete many of those records, so she wasn't sure how much there would be to find there.

Sola stood outside of Jamus' abode, pondering her options. She was leaning towards the Dirt Port, mostly due to its proximity, when her tab vibrated in her pocket. She pulled it out and saw a brief message from Meenshin.

"M. missing. Crew turning station upside down looking for her. No leads."

A moment's panic siezed Sola's heart as she felt a mix of fear and fury sweep through her. As she knew all too well from resolving others' mysteries, suspecting something carries very different emotional weight from having your fears confirmed. She wanted to punch somebody, to break something painful. This case was no longer about some foreigner scientist in over his head or even about preventing the creation of some apocalyptic bioweapon. This was personal. 

She immediately headed towards the Port in a steady jog rather than her usual brisk walk.

***

The blue-eyed detective was in such an anxious such that she had fully entered the Dirt Port Quarantine Lab before noticing the large green serpent that was the room's sole occupant at the moment, curved over a work bench peering into a microscope.

*Beeshelon!* The Inquirer skidded to a panicked stop. *No, wait...* A Bayhatile, yes, but not the Invetelic representative. This person had alternating bright blue and yellow spirals painted down their length, rather than Beeshelon's dull red. Not a traditional Bayhatile familial or status symbol, but a modern innovation for someone who has found themselves outside of Bayhatile society. 

"One moment... neighbor." The large, slitted eyes remain focused down the microscope.

Sola knew this person, one of the non-human minority residents of Utopia, though she was struggling to recall their name. *They moved here only a couple of years back.*

Thankfully, the languid nature of the serpent race was on full display, giving the Inquirer plenty of time to trawl her memory, all while second-guessing her decision to come here instead of the Medical Cooperative. Under these circumstances, she had little patience for the relaxed pace of Bayhatile conversation. At least she had evidently arrived at a low traffic time for the Quarantine Lab.

Eventually, those large eyes left the microscope and turned to face the visitor. The face that held the eyes was a dark green, far darker than Beeshelon's, almost bordering on a grey. The head that held that face began rotating in greeting before freezing in embarrassment and transitioning into a nod. Sola chose to return the initial Bayhatile greeting, rotating her head in her best approximation of the gesture.

"Ah... Inquirer Sola... how good it is to see you." Their upbeat tone indicated this was genuine sentiment rather than a mere pleasantry.

"And you as well, Teeaphalon." Sola had remembered the name at the last possible moment, her memory driven by desperation to the correct answer. "What are you doing here? Last we met, you seemed to be happily involved with the Medical Cooperative."

Teeaphalon nodded slowly and unblinkingly. "Indeed it was so... but then Etion passed."

*That's right.* Sola recalled *that* unfortunate accident just a three or four weeks back. She had intended to go to the replanting ceremony, but had been too hungover from the Rootbound Weevil's crushing defeat at the hands of the Mid-Trunk Revolutionaries. 

"The Port asked if anyone... had analytical medical experience... We thought that..." They shook the tip of their tail twice in mild annoyance. "Ah... that a 'change of pace' might be nice... But enough about us... how can we help you?"

The uncharacteristic cutting to the point caught Sola off guard, but she wasn't about to question her good fortune, given the urgency of the situation. She quickly reached into an interior pocket of her coat and pulled forth the small bag, bloody ceramic within. 

"Am hoping you could tell me something about the source of this blood." She held out the bag.

Teeaphalon slithered closer and looked closely at the minute piece of evidence.

"The Port recently purged... most of our... individualized records... we're afraid."

Sola's lips tightened. "I'm aware," she said wryly.

"We will gladly... do what we can... neighbor." Their long, forked tongue slipped into a pocket in the small, vest-like garment worn about a fifth of the way down their body. A moment later, the pink tongue emerged, now encased in a glove of sorts. 

Sola handed off the bag to that tongue and Teeaphalon deftly extracted the sample as they moved across the room. Gone was the usual langor, they were not acting with swiftness and precision, a clear professional at home in this space, despite their short tenure here. The sample was dropped into a vial of some clear liquid that fizzed slightly upon impact. The vial was in turn placed in a non-nondescript, off-white machine, the face of which was covered in a multitude of buttons. A few quick key presses later and the device began whirring softly. A faintly acrid, faintly sour smell reached Solas and she wrinkled her nose.

*I've been spending entirely too much time around lab chemicals of late,* she thought. *This is what... the third time today?*

But then there was a gentle chime. The machine ceased rumbling and and text began running across a nearby monitor. Teeaphalon studied the output intently. 

"The blood... is human... of Silphite extraction... though the microbial and genetic fragments... indicate that the subject... is well traveled. They are likely most recently arrived... from Xionia... or perhaps one of its moons... We would need a large sample... to be sure."

The Bayhatile technician retrieved the vial from the machine, placed it in a fridge, and disposed of the tongue glove before turning to look at the Inquirer. 

Sola was staring at the ceiling though, lost in thought. This stretched on long enough that most humans would have interrupted with a trite, "You okay?" or even a "Station to Sola?" Teeaphalon was more respectful of a good mid-conversation ponder, however. After waiting silently for a long while, they returned to their microscope, that dextrous tongue occasionally darting out to adjust one dial or another.

Sola's mind, meanwhile, was in overdrive, fitting pieces together. The Xionian bit was odd. Xionia was a the capital of a reasonably powerful polity neighboring the Invetelic Empire and currently at wary peace with that larger neighbor. It was odd that they would seek to take such violent action against a person *fleeing* the Invetelics. This oddity was not of primary importance to her, however. Instead, she was thinking about the fact that his was *not* the third time she had been in a lab today, but the *second*. She had only thought it was the third because she had smelled biological media in Aberseth's ransacked room. And that faint olfactory memory tied back to the fourth place she had encountered that scent recently: on the smuggler who had slammed into her outside Dirt Port back when this whole mess started. 

A man who had recognized a Silphite gesture by sound alone.

Two conclusions leapt into hte Inquirer's mind simultaneously, each fighting for priority.

- The smuggler/spy/kidnapper wasn't just trying to retrieve research data. He was trying to operationalize it.
- He hadn't bumped into her by accident.

*Fuck, fuck, fuck!* Sola hurridly pulled her dark blue coat off and began frantically looking through the pockets. Sure enough her fingers soon found a small, out-of-place, metallic object. Holding it up to the lab's bright lights, she recognized it at once. A transmitter.

As the longstanding Inquirer of Utopia, Sola was more used to solving mysteries, crimes, and conspiracies than being the object of one. Even so, she felt *exceptionally* foolish at the moment. The man she was hunting had, in fat, been tracking her every movement for days. That's how he had known Métier's usual haunts. And it meant that he knew where she had dropped off Aberseth.

*I might as well trip on a Root and fall off into the black for all the good I've been!* Sola let the transmitter fall to the ground and hastily re-donned her coat before sprinting out the door without a work of thanks or farewell to Teeaphalon.

The Bayhatile looked up from their microscope when the door closed automatically behind the fleeing Inquirer. They flicked their tongue out twice, tasting the air and confirming that Sola was gone. Then they blinked slowly thrice in an expression of pleasurable pride. 

"It... feels good to be... of use to our neighbors." Having uttered this with complete confidence, the technician returned to their microscope.

***

Sola was just beginning to grow winded when she turned onto Entisol Lane towards Mino's apothecary. She increased her pace upon passing a pair of medics, clad in their customary black long-sleeved shirts and pants festooned with pockets and pouches, carrying an occupied stretcher towards the emergency clinic. The dashing of the Inquirer's hopes came along with a certain grim resolve.

She bounded up the final steps to the apothecary and shoved past clumps of neighbors who varied in their degree of helpfulness and voyeurism. As she did so, she could hear Mino's booming voice alternating between directing clean up efforts and fending off some intervention from a concerned medic.

"Don't handle any of that without nitrile gloves — It's just a flesh wound, I'll come down to the clinic later — Stop! Leave that entire corner alone! I have no clue what Harron was working on over there."

Immediately after a strong wave of conflicting, though all generally acrid, smells slammed into Sola's face: a hodge-podge of chemical vapors, the harsh tang of burning metal, the distinct scent of smokeless power, and slight hit of blood. Finally the scene itself resolved into view. Sola stepped in and just to the side of the doorway to avoid obstructing the stream of Utopians going in and out while she observed the situation. Shattered glass from labware was strewn across counters and floors. Pools of indistinct liquids turned the room into a chemical minefield. The emergency fans were running, sucking vapors of the room and adding some acoustic chaos to the scene. Mino was sitting in a high chair up against the far wall and bellowing out orders to the many people coming and going. He was shirtless, his thick, dark chest hair on full display and bloody bandage wrapping his right shoulder. A black-clad medic with a frizzy tussle of blonde hair — Katio was the name, Sola believed — stood over the proprietor, clearly distressed by their patient's continued presence in the smashed up apothecary.

Mino noticed Sola almosted immediately and waved her over.

"Step that way. To your right. No, further. Look out for that boronic acid over there. Been waiting for you to get here."

Thankfully Mino had worked with Sola on multiple occasions in the past and so was prepared for the ensuing barrage of questions as Sola entered full high Inquirer.

"Flashbang, chemical astringint, old fashioned projectile handgun?" Only the slightest of uplight at the end indicated this was question rather than a statement, but Mino caught it and nodded.

"aye. Acytelenic-IV, if I had to guess. Had us hacking something fierce 'til the fans kicked in."

"Fatalities?"

"None, yet, thank the Tree. Uma and Hasheen are in critical condition."

"How many assailants?"

"Just the one, came in ablazing, grabbing your friend and was out before we knew what happened. Sorry, Sola, we're used to folks trying to steal chems, not people while shooting up the place."

Sola ignored the apology. Now was not the time to be casting blame and she certainly wasn't faultless herself in this situation.

"Description?"

"After a flashbang and acytelnic? I ain't a miracle worker."

Sola's lips tightened in frustration. A high voice chirped up from back across the room.

"I saw them!" Sola turned to see a furry creature, maybe a meter long, jumping from counter to counter. Eight short legs expertly redirected their momentum with each bounce to avoid broken glass and spilled liquid. Lessurans looked somewhat like a cross between a ferret and a millipede, though altogether too large for either. A triad of them took up residence in Utopia about the same time as Sola and had soon settled down just a few buildings down the street from Mino and his own two partners. Soon both groups were engaging in apparent competitive childrearing. Mino *et al.* called it quits at four, but the Lessurans had kept going all the way to thirteen. This brown and tan stripped one was their eldest child, Heetopanto, the up-and-coming apothecary lieutenant of Mino's lab.

With a final bound, Heetopanto landed on the one clean spot of the nearest counter and stood up on his hindmost legs, bringing his four black eyes just about level with the Inquirer's two blue ones.

"I was returning from an errand just as they were leaving with the new guy at gunpoint. Sorry for not chasing them, I was worried about everyone inside here."

Sola gave a curt nod. "Correct. Description?"

"They are human. Wearing a breather mask though, so didn't see their face. Black vest with a pointy collar and curved zipper than ran from here to here." Heetopanto gestured from the center of his neck down in a leftward curving arc to the approximate equivalent of a human waist.

*That's Xionia military style, but they wear exclusively wear blue. Perhaps someone whose aesthetic sensibilities lean Xionian paramilitary then.*

"They were about your height," the Lessuran continued. "Similar hair. And they move the same way you do."

This caught Sola by surprise. "What?"

The Lessuran lifted six paws in what appeared to be mimicry of a human shrug. "Humans don't all move the same. You don't move like most Utopians. You don't even move like other Silphites like Madam. But you do move like the *entuvanapa* who did all this. Don't know what else to tell you."

Sola was unfamiliar with the word *entuvanapa* but assumed from context that it was a Lessuran slur. "Madam" would refer to Métier. Some non-Silphite cultures, non-humans in particular, sometimes assumed that the title was her first name. Sola was concentrating hard, trying to figure out how to interpret this information.

*This 'movement' isn't an off-station thing or even a Silphite thing, apparently. Maybe it stems from some training I had?* A brief memory came to mind. Her father, a Silphite cop, teaching her and her two siblings how to handle themselves. Footwork, throws, punches, her face hitting the dirt time and time again. Then she was back in the present.

"Why no station-wide emergency alert yet?"

A new medic who had recently entered answered. "There is, outside the block."

Sola's eyebrows furrowed. "The block?"

Mino answered  that one. "Communications block. Came down just before the attack. Definitely the immediate neighborhood, maybe the entire Roots. We already sent for engineers to work on it. I —" Mino coughed briefly. "doubt it was coincidental."

"Concur." Sola's mind was moving from evidence collection to action. She dropped her voice to avoid causing panic. "Mino, if a recent arrival needed equipment and supplies to synthesize a novel virus, where on the station would they go?"

A mix of confusion, surprise, and alarm showed on the faces of all those in earshot. Mino swallowed the implications swiftly though and responded directly. "Yeah. My expertise is more on the chemistry side of things that biology, but I have a sense of what would be required and where to get it."

Sola pulled her tablet out, taped it a few times, and handed it to the shirtless apothecary. "Draw up a list of locations and materiel."

Mino immediately handed it to the hovering medic. "Make yourself useful, Katio. I dicate you type." He gestured at his injured shoulder in explanation.

The Inquirer had already turned to Heetopanto, who was readying to bound away towards some clean-up task.

"Wait!" The Lessuran froze and twisted back to look at her. "You're family still live down the street?"

"Yes?" His head titled distinctly further than would be comfortable for a human.

"Are your siblings home/ One's old enough to run all over the station?"

Realization started to dawn on his snout. Heetopanto blinked each pair of eyes in sequence. "Probably... six?"

"Fetch them now. As long as commos are out, we're going to split up and visit all the place Mino gives us. We ask if they have seen anyone who matches your description come in for equipment or supplies."

***

There were eight potential places from which the kidnapper may have acquired the necessary scientific equipment. They were spread from deep in the Roots to the tip of the Crown, near Sky Port. Sola assigned herself to the closest one. She had already worn herself running here and she wanted to conserve her strength for whatever was yet to come. Plus Lessurans were far faster than humans when they desired, at least over the distances involved here.

There was also Sola's suspicion  that their target had gone to ground someone in this very part of the Roots. So much of this case was concentrated around here and she couldn't image that the kidnapper would have tried to move either Métier or Aberseth far on this crowded station.

Unfortunately, the first stop was a bust. Tabitha Floren could account for all of the machines and materials that had come through the Wayward Chemist — none of them were in the hands of someone who matched Heetopanto's description in the slightest.

Just as the Inquirer was stepping out of the Chemist, a many-legged curry creature came sprinting up to her. They skidded to a stop, gasping for breath, with wild excitement shining in all their eyes. 

*Damn, Lessurans are speedy!* This was one of Heetopanto's younger sisters, though Sola wasn't sure they had ever been formally introduced. While old enough to be entrusted with this errand, she was still young enough to clearly think of all this as some exciting game. 

"Samu... over... at... Oro.. Oru... Ouroboros... saw him." Each syllable was separated by several breaths, more than quick enough to be called hyperventilation in a human. Sola suppressed a desire to shake her and demand that she spit it out already. But that was just impatient fear driving her. The kid was already going as fast as she could.

"Samu... helped move... the heavy stuff... to a place nearby." Every muscle in Sola's body tensed as the Lessuran finally got the address out. It took everything he had to avoid sprinting off at once. But there remained an important preparatory step.

"You are magnificent and *so* fast. Your parents are going to be so very proud of you." The child, starting to recover herself, spun in a tight circle of pleasure at the praise. Sola pressed on. "I need you to do one more thing, as quick as you can."

"But I'm tired!" The Lessuran adolescent rolled over on her back, her eight legs all going limp simultaneously. 

Sola restrained her exasperation an kept her tone cheerful as she bent down to one knee. "Only the fastest one can do this job." Now to appeal to self-interest as well as pride. "If you do this, I'll get you whatever treat you want on the station. Your parents don't even need to know about it."

In an instant, her volunteer errand girl was back on her feet and rearing up to match eye level with the kneeling Inquirer.

"Promise?" A conspiratorial glee shown in her eyes. Part of having a dozen siblings mean rarely getting something special just to yourself.

"Promise." No high-pitched baby talk here. Sola said this with complete seriousness, hoping the desperation didn't show in her voice

"Alright, tell me what to do!"

***

The specified address was non-descript enough. A small, plain, gray, utilitarian warehouse, tucked into an inconvenient spot on the outer ring of the Roots. The heavier gravity of the region dragged on Sola's tired frame and gave each step a certain weighty finality. No doubt the inconvenience of the place was why it had been laying empty until now. Sola thought it probably hadn't seen much use since the station was built. While Utopia did informally recognize something approximating private property when it came to one's residence and workplace, any unused spot was generally considered up for grabs. No one would have objected to an outsider showing up and moving lab equipment in here.

Night was falling on the quadrant, not that this place ever received much of the central light from the Tree. It was too far out and down amid the Roots. Most visibility instead came from the streetlights embedded in the floor on either side of the path. Sola stood a bit down the street, assessing the layout. No windows on the front, just a standard door and an industrial loading dock, much smaller than its planet-side equivalents due to the scarcity of vehicles on the station. One of the Roots was wrapped around the side and back of the building. That would likely mean a secondary entrance on the one remaining side, opposite from where Sola stood now, though there was also possibly a vertical emergency exit to a lower level of the station, if such a lower level even existed. A quick glance over the railing of the street between two buildings confirmed there was indeed such a level, though it was purely maintenance acceess with no other buildings. She couldn't tell if the warehouse had a stair leading down that way.

The Inquirer chewed her lower lip. She would prefer to do some more prolonged surveillance before moving in, but every minute that passed decreased the likelihood of her finding Métier or Aberseth alive.

*Nothing for it.* At the very least, she no longer had the tracker on her. There was a small chance she might catch the kidnapper unawares. *And the Tree could start sprouting fruit, I suppose.* She performed one last check of her weapons, spit on the path for luck, and crept towards the warehouse.

She opted for the side entrance, in the alley. As was the Utopian norm, the door lacked any kind of apparent lock, though the new occupant could have easily installed a crude deadbolt or equivalent on the inside. Sola took a moment to regularize her breathing, then reached out to the handle. She turned as slowly and silently as she could manage. No resistance.

*Now for the moment of truth.* Sola pressed her right shoulder up against he door and tested to see if it would open. *Not deadbolt either*. The door felt a bit heavier than she expected, but that could have just been a function of the increased gravity here on the outskirts of the station. The Inquirer quickly opened the door just enough to permit her to slip through.

If the street outside was dim, the inside of the warehouse was intensely dark. A weak glow came from the cracked door behind her. A few sparse skylights above meagerly supplement the light, but were facing towards the interior of the station and the entire Root district, so no stars were able to supplement the illumination. A table — more of a counter really — stood a couple of meters away. Blocky silhouettes  of indeterminate lab machines loomed up from that table. The familiar smell of biological media and solvents washed over her. The rest of the room lay in impenetrable darkness.

Sola took another step inside, following the wall. A faint noise that conceivably could have been a groan came from somewhere deeper in the room. The Inquirer froze, listening intently. Long seconds of silence elapsed. Nothing. Another step. Her hand found the light control panel. She flipped a switch and bright light flooded the room, momentarily blinding her. At that instant, a wet, nauseatingly sweet smelling cloth closed over her mouth and nose. For just a moment, she saw an improvised lab filing a small portion of the cavernous space. She also saw seemed to be two human figures bound to chairs. She threw an elbow back at her assailant but before it landed, she had slipped into unconsciousness.

***
 The Inquirer came to with a rgaging headache, one of an altogether different variety than the alcohol-induced hangovers she was all too familiar with. Before her vision came into focus, she recognized several basic facts about her situation.

One. She was bound to a run-of-the-mill, printed plasticon chair, her hands tied behind her and each leg to a chair leg. The chair was light enough to be easily moved, but durable enough that she wouldn't be able to break it by slamming herself onto the hard floor. She flexed a bit, testing the improvised plastic cord bindings. They were well tied, with little play, but also not so tight as to cut off her circulation. Not the work of an amateur.

Two. Her blue coat was gone, which meant her gun with it. The way the cords dug into her calves, she suspected her baton was gone as well. She couldn't immediately tell if her knuckles or knife were still on her person. The latter would be worth checking for when she had the chance. 

Three. She hadn't been unconscious for long. Her throat wasn't parched and her limbs didn't yet have the soreness of being bound in an uncomfortable position for an extended period of time.

Four and most importantly. She was alive. This wasn't to be taken for granted, as her assailant could have easily slit her throat instead of tying her up. Evidently she was still needed for something. A hostage for bargaining with Utopia? Or did they think she might have the password to Aberseth's data?

The first thing she was able to see was the inside of the main entrance to the warehouse, only a few meter in front of her and just to the right. It had been heavily reinforced and seemed to be sealed shut. If her captor had gone through such efforts on that door, she can't have imagined they had overlooked the side entrance she had used. That meant it hadn't been merely unlocked. It had been a trap. One that she had walked right into.

Sola shoved aside her rising sense of frustrating impotence and continued her appraisal. Sensing breathing to her right, she turned her head to see Métier similarly bound a couple of meters away. Aberseth too, just beyond the tailor. Métier's exposed arms, neck, and face showed signs of torture, but no permanent injuries and nothing that risked killing her. Once again, not an amateur. Sola's sidelined since of shame evaporated into anger at the sight of her friend in such a condition.

The tailor's head had been sagging forward, her eyes closed, but evidently she was not sleeping, as she stirred at the sound of the Inquirer's slight motions. She blinked a few times but once she saw Sola's face, she instantly became alert, her gray eye's locking onto Sola's. No defeat or resignation could be seen in those eyes. 

Without preamble, Métier uttered a name. "Aster Trabahan." 

Sola's blood went cold. Métier shouldn't — *couldn't* — know that name. That name had been dead and buried decades ago. What bearing could it have here?

But the deductive part of her mind began making connections behind the back of her protesting conscious self. Heetopanto's description. The Silphite blood sample. The smuggler at the port. Memories from much further in her past. This line of thought had hardly left the starting blocks when a low, cruel laugh filled the warehouse.

"In the flesh." The voice was that raspy, scarred voice of the smugger in his waves of fabric. Sola's head snapped around as the source of the voice stepped in front of her. He bent down before his captive Inquirer, deep blue eyes meeting deep blue eyes. His face was much changed by long years and many trials endured, but it was perfectly recognizable to her.

"Hello, sister."

***

Sola had been a teenager when the Invetlics had conquered Silphus, adding her home planet to their nascent Empire. While she would later learn of the death and misery that this brought to her people, in the near term her life changed little.

Both of her parents were police officers in a minor city. The new rulers had need of law enforcement just as, indeed more than, the previous government. Most of the serious unrest — and the Invetlics initial efforts at consolidating control — were concentrated far way in capitals and economic hubs. News of these activities was attenuated by distance and censored media. Her parents had always viewed themselves as apolitical and saw little reason to alter that stance now. So Sola saw the flags at school change and the portraits of elected officials come down at her parents' workplace, but otherwise her childhood continued largely unaltered. 

Two years later she enrolled in the local police academy under the proud supervision of her parents. There she excelled beyond their wildest hopes, soon finding herself on a path, not towards the life of a provincial beat cop like her parents, but towards a detective career in Apenus, the capital city of the Silphite Province of the Invetelic Empire.

Sola had few qualms about this path, other than moving so far from her parents and younger sister. She had been raised to believe in law and order, a black and white view of right and wrong. Though, to be honest, she only really believed in order and even that was more out of an aesthetic sensibility than a philosophical stance. Ever since she was young, she cherished neatness and organization. Her passion was solving puzzles of all kinds, wrapping her mind around a problem, putting each piece in its place until the singular, unambiguously correct solution came into view.

She approached her detective  work in much the same way. Evidence, testimony, motives, lies, and half-truths, all these were just pieces to be examined and sorted. She had only limited interest in the innumerable criminals and victims and bystanders she interacted as people. And so, she paid little heed to as punishments for crines were increased, as civil liberties were first suspended and then abandoned altogether, and as more and more of her cases dealt with political matters. These trends only mattered to the extent that they impacted her job, whether they made her puzzles easier or harder to solve.

That's the story she had tried to tell herself at the time. What she continued to insist to herself as her few childhood friends grew increasingly nervous around her or disappeared altogether. As the tenor of first Apenus and then her hometown grew paranoid and afraid. As her visits home were increasing filled with shouting matches between her older brother and her parents.

Her brother Aster had been a college student when the invasion occurred. Similarly bright to Sola, he had been the first to leave home, to study biology at a prestigious university in Apenus. To her parents displeasure, he had soon picked up political ideas there — a key reason why they had directed their next child, Sola, towards the police academy rather than university. Sola respected her brother immensely. Where she had spent her childhood focused on solitary pursuit of puzzles, he had maintained a robust social life without sacrificing his intellectual pursuits. That said, she could hardly see the point in the arguments he came home with, about complicity and solidarity and compliance with their oppressors and other theoretical concepts. She could have at least understood his interest had they still lived in a democracy, but now? But then again, many interests of other people didn't make sense to her, so who was she to judge? 

That said, Sola held a secret pride in her brother for the strength of his convictions. She, who hardly believed anything and mostly just kept her head down, sometimes found herself wishing she could believe in something, anything, like he did. Still, she was grateful that Aster kept such opinions to himself outside the home. She had seen folks arrested for less and he had such a promising career as a biologist underway. There was little chance of Aster being able to do anything to overthrow the Invetelic occupation. She would hate to see her idolized older sibling throw his future away on something so futile. 

She kept a close, protective eye on him in Apenus, where they both lived and worked close by to one another. And by all appearances, he, like her, kept his head down and toed the line in his professional and personal life.

It was thus to her complete astonishment when Sola, backed by an entire brigade of armored police, led the bust of a nationalist cell and found herself face-to-face with Aster. It was supposed to be the biggest success of her career thus far. She had linked a series of anonymous assassinations and bombings to a particular revolutionary group. She had uncovered their network of safe houses and supply lines. She had identified when and where their leadership would be meeting next. She had done what dozens of other Silphite investigators —and the Invetelic Security Service — had failed to do. This was about to be the proudest moment in her life. 

Instead she saw not only her brother hooded and taken away, but soon her parents and even her now college-aged younger sister. The Invetelics were strong believers in familial loyalty and collective punishment. Sola was only spared herself because she had indisputably been the one responsible for solving the case. Even so, she recognized the resulting commendation and invitation to join the Security Service for what they were: a test of loyalty and a short leash that she would spend the rest of her life on.

She had taken the extra vacation days that had come along with her commendation to return to her parents' house — her house, she supposed, knowing it would never feel like home again. She sat alone, in the dark, in her childhood meditation room. 

For three days, she thought over all the steps that had brought her here. All the arrests, imprisonments, and executions that she had played a crucial role in bringing about. All the shattered families left behind, often without even the funerary teeth of their slain loved ones, just as she was now. She thought about all the injustices that she had facilitated, the tyranny she had been complicit in, the orders she had so eagerly complied with. Now, she was forced to recognize that all of Aster's theoretical politics were not theoretical at all. They were the stuff of real choices she had made and the consequences she was responsible for.

On the fourth day, she thought about the obtuseness — no, the selfishness — that had enabled her to ignore all of this until it impacted her directly. A conviction did not require clear intent, not if the perpetrator had willfully ignored the predictable consequences of their actions. She may not be guilty under Invetelic law, but she was certainly guilty by any reasonable ethical standard. She thought long and hard about what her sentence should be.

The answer was obvious to her: the same ignoble, anonymous death that she had brought upon her family. So she spent the next several hours meticulously planning the assassination of the highest ranking member of the secret police that she could conceivably reach, a target that Aster's crew never could have hoped to reach. She didn't have to escape afterwards. She didn't want to escape. That was the point.

Once she had the plan all worked out though, she realized she couldn't do it. She was no revolutionary like Aster. He had died for a cause greater than him, for something he believed righteous. She held no such belief. She was acting out of revenge and self-pity. She couldn't lay claim to a revolutionary's death. That was an honor she didn't deserve.

On the fifth day, she drank herself past inebriation. She laid her service weapon upon the table and started at it. She picked up the gun three times over the course of that day. Once it made it as far as her lips. But when the day ended, she was still alive.

On the sixth day, she packed a small bug full of mostly cash and booze. Then she walked out the door.

***

Now bound and captive in a warehouse on a backwater station floating in space light years from that home, Sola felt it all over again: her childhood happiness, the love for her brother, the pain of the loss of her family, and the guilt at her responsibility. She also relived all the fear and excitement of the past several days here on Utopia, hunting a shadow from her past. Out of that hurricane of emotion, she could only extract a whispered: "You're alive."

Aster's worn face nodded slowly, seriously. He looked far older than the couple of years he had on his sister.

"I hadn't planned on making you a part of this, Solaterra." He enunciated her name slowly, distinctly, as if uttering a holy prayer. Or a terrible curse. "I saw little point in avenging myself upon you. But you were always good at solving mysteries. You think I would have learned that by now."

Sola's mind was in turmoil, unsure how to respond or even what to think, but some small part of her, the part that craved order and made her a detective, clung to the pragmatic. Her hands flashed a sign behind the chair. Métier, sitting next to her and looking at the face-off between the two siblings, caught it and signed a confirmation in return. 

"How?" Sola continued to whisper, as if too loud of a noise might shatter her reality.

Aster closed his eyes for several seconds. When he opened them, a fierce hatred shown in those undulled blue eyes, a hatred directly equally inwards and outwards. 

"How? How? How do you think, dear sister?" This last was said with a sneer. "By selling out everyone I knew, the guilty and the innocent alike. By doing everything those snakes told me to. By helping them hunt down as many of my comrades in arms as I could lay my hands on and then hunting down anyone else the snakes called an enemy."

Aster laughed bitterly. "Turns out a certain proficiency for the hunt runs in the blood. " The snakes decided I was too useful to throw away. So now I deal with any mess they don't want to soil their slithering bellies on."

"Even if it means working for a bioweapons manufacturer?"There was more venom in that question than Sola intended. The crumbling idolization of her revolutionary hero brother cut her more deeply than she realized.

Aster stood back up and looked at her quizzically. "You think I work for Beesheelon? That idiot?" She seemed genuinely offended by the accusation. "Of course not. I wouldn't work for someone so dumb as to think they could hide the escape of their prized scientist — and his research data — from the Security Service."

Their captor folded his hands behind his back and began pacing back and forth before the three prisoners. It pained Sola to see him move like that, just has he used to when arguing with their parents.

"Out of respect for our familial ties and your frankly obsessive need to solve every puzzle, I will tell you what is about to happen. I have the encrypted drive. I have the hardware key. All I need is the password. One of you has it. I am going to hurt you three until I get it. The tailor has proven resistant to physical pressure. I suspect my sister is as well. But I'l bet both of you will talk to save each other from what I'm about to do." He paused his pacing and glanced at Aberseth. "As for you, I've hardly touched him yet and he's almost done." The pacing resumed. "Whoever has the password will talk."

Aster paced in silence for a moment, allowing his words to sink in. Finally he came to halt in front of Métier and turned to face all three prisoners. "Once I have the password, I will make all  this look like the work of Xionian agents — you like my outfit?" He gestured at the Xionian paramilitary attire he now wore. "The kidnapping of a government scientist, that's all the excuse my superiors need to declare war."

He paused at the confused look on all three faces of his captive audience. "Ah, yes. You probably haven't heard out in this backwater. The war with the Severans is at am impasse. Both sides are looking to wrap things up with a rather unsatisfactory truce.  The Invetelic higher-ups want to open a new front — the war machine must keep turning and all that."

Sola considered his words just as she would any other piece of evidence — and found it sorely wanting.

"Bullshit." Her whisper was gone. This was the stern and direct tone of the Inquirer of Utopia. Aster's eyebrows shot up as Sola pressed on.

"You hate the Invetelics far too much to passively do their bidding all these years. Your leash is long. Long enough that you were sent here, outside of Invetelic jurisdiction, alone. Long enough that you know the contents of what Aberseth was working on rather than being given simple retrieval instructions. Long enough that you could have easily taken to the black and never looked back." Sola was caught up in the puzzle now, her inferences coming tumbling out of her. "And you wouldn't need the lab equipment here if you just wanted to frame the Xionians for kidnapping."

Aberseth seemed to feel something other than fear for the first time since Sola's arrival. His head craned to look over at the lab equipment. "

A slow smile was spreading across Aster's face. He began nodding slowly. "I certainly could have run. Further than you did, sister. And to somewhere much nicer than this dump of a station." Patriotic indignation flared on the face of both Sola and Métier. Aster waved a hand in dismissal. "But I interrupted. Do go on."

Sola couldn't resist the opportunity. "I don't think you happened to be the one assigned to this job. You had been monitoring Beesheelon and her lab prior to Aberseth's escape. You have enough microbiology knowledge to know what you were looking at. You saw an opportunity. You got your hands on most of the information necessary to weaponize Aberseth's work once it was completed."

Aberseth spoke up now. "I can tell from here that you're using Hyancin's archaeogenetic splicing technique. Unless you're *really* into studying the evolutionary process of RNA in hazardous environments, you're synthesizing a custom virus." 

That was it then. Sola had indeed figured it out. "You don't intend to frame this as an Xionian kidnapping. You intend to build a virus and unleash it *on* the Xionians —"

"One of the *moons* of Xionia," Aster corrected. "Don't want to damage their warfighting capacity overly much."

"—on one of the moons then," Sola continued, "You'll also release clear evidence that the attack was of Invetelic origin. The entire Argentum will take up arms against the Invetelics for developing such a bioweapon."

Aster began clapping loudly and slowly. The resulting echos resounded across the largely empty room. "Bravo! Bravo! My sister the detective, everyone! That first story I spun you were merely my orders. My actual plans are indeed as you have supposed." Aster's hands returned to behind his back. "You know, it's somewhat comforting to know that I got caught all those years ago by someone like you. God forbid I had been fool enough to get caught by... what was his name? That oafish colleague of yours at the time?"

"Patton." Sola answered with great reluctance, not appreciating Aster's mockery.

"Yes! Patton! Anyways, congratulations on yet another case solved. Too bad it doesn't change anything for you." A knife suddenly appeared in Aster's hand, sparkling in the overhead lights. Aberseth yelped and began rocking in his chair. "Now, password. I'm willing to be lenient if you don't waste my time."

Métier finally spoke up, her voice sarcastic. "Let me guess. You will let whoever gives you the password live?"

"Of course not, Madam," Aster said with mock deference. He bent down in the tailor's face and brought the tip of the knife to her cheek. Sola stiffened in fear and fury. "I would never assume such gullibility on your part. You all know far too much to remain alive. What I can promise is a quick and painless death in return for compliance."

"We will never help a murderous traitor such as you!" Métier finished by spitting in Aster's face, the sudden motion causing the knife to prick her cheek. A drop of blood ran down her face.

Aster calmly stood back up and wiped his own face with a sleeve. "Me? A traitor? I have done terrible things, but I am still loyal to the cause. I thought you would understand that. I am about to be responsible for the downfall of an empire. What have you accomplished, hiding out in your treehouse, palling it up with the real traitor here!"

Aster paused when his words were met with a confused expression on the tailor's face. Sola felt her stomach drop and everything grow distant. Her brother must have noticed some of this because he began looking back and forth between the pair, eye's sparking with malevolent mischief. 

"Oh? What's this? The famed spymaster Métier does not know? You didn't tell her, dear sister? Not after twenty-five years in exile together?"

Sola's mouth was dry, her tongue suddenly far too large and clumsy to attempt a response. Panic filled her. This was not how she wanted Métier to learn of her past.

"Well then, allow me." Aster turned gleefully back to the tailor. "My sister was the lead police detective for the Invetelic government on Silphus. A genuine protigy, she was only in her twenties and she had taken down countless revolutionaries — your and my comrades, Métier. You must have known some of them. The Sons of the Salamander. The GYKB. The Externo Eleven. The Student Assembly for a Just Society. Shall I go on?"

Each of these names had been like a slap across Métier's face. Her chin remained high, though her eye's showed doubt and betrayal. Sola, meanwhile, hung her head in shame. Aster was exaggerating her importance; only on the Salamanders case, the one that led to his arrest, was Sola the lead detective. She had certainly been complicit in the others, however, as she had been in the imprisonment or execution of so many other individuals her brother hand left unnamed. Whatever judgement dealt upon her was warranted.

Finally Métier turned her eyes upon her longtime friend.

"You. Fascist. Pig." She said each word distinctly. "To think that I called you friend. I wish I had gutted you in the dark of  that hold a quarter century back. I would have gladly dwelt in your blood for those long weeks journeying here."

The tailor pressed, continuing in this vein and condemning Sola to damnation. So well did Métier's words match Sola's inner thoughts that it wasn't until several invectives into this diatribe that she realized something was off. The Inquirer looked up to meet Métier's gaze. The tailor seemed furious, certainly. It was all too apparent in her flinty eyes, the scrunched brows, the sneer of the upper lip, the venom laced in each word. But Sola had known her now perhaps ex-friend for half of her life. Métier was a a good actor, but at this moment she *was* acting.

Sola hoped her surprise read as in response to the viciousness of the attack. The Inquirer gathered her wits for a moment before leaping into the fray, interrupting Métier's ongoing insults.

"So what, you pompous coward! I was doing my job, trying to save lives from scumbags hiding in the dark and detonating bomb!" She hated herself for saying this, but pressed on. " I was on the front lines, fighting for something. You — You meanwhile snuck away as soon as things got hard! Now you play act the revolutionary while others do the real work! You're a fake! A pretender!" Her argument was hardly consistent but she hoped that would be attributed to the heat of the moment.

Aster's face shown with vindictive pleasure at their performance. Aberseth meanwhile, did his best to shrink down in his chair with terrified confusion.

Métier managed a sort of hop-slide to bring her chair around to face Sola directly. As she did so, the Inquirer noticed that Métier's feet were not bound near as tightly her own. Was that a mistake on Aster's part or had the tailor managed to loosen them somehow? Thankfully, the captor didn't appear to notice and Métier resumed her verbal assault.

"I'm the coward?" I'm the one who ran away? If it hadn't been for me, you would have drunk yourself to death six months after disembarking here. God, I wish I had let you!"

"Fuck you!" Sola's chair was was now turned towards her interlocutor. 

"Even now you're a pig, incapable of the thought required to put together a coherent insult. 'Fuck you!' That's all you have?"

"At least people can understand what I say! All your big words and theory that don't amount to anything! Maybe if you tried talking like normal people, you'd actually get some folks to join the revolution!"

This went on for several minutes before Aster stepped in between the two bound women. While he clearly was enjoying watching his sister's friendship come crashing apart, the shouting match was distracting from his goal. 

"Shut up, both of you, or I'll starting cutting pieces off!"

Sola choked off the next insult that was on her tongue with more than a little regret. She was awfully proud of this one and thought that Métier would have enjoyed hearing it. Métier similarly seemed poised to continue lobbing insults, her mouth agape, but she restrained herself and opted for simply spitting on the ground. Whether this was intended to be a sign of disrespect towards Sola, Aster, or both was unclear.

Pleased at the responsiveness to his command, Aster turned towards the Inquirer and the shaking Aberseth, his back to the tailor. "Enough catching up, sis. It's time to cough up the encryption key. Which one of you —" He went crashing down on the ground. His head smacked the metal floor hard. Métier, still tied to her chair at the waist and arms was atop him. Evidently her legs were now completely freed of their fetters. The next moment, a harsh wail filled the room. It took several seconds for Sola to realize that it was her brother screaming as he flailed about, trying to shove his assailant off of him.

Eventually he was successful, stumbling to his feet and leaving the still bound tailor lying on her side, her legs trashing as she tried to right herself. Aster looked startled, stunned even, his face pale. He clumsily clutched at his neck with both hands. There was blood there, lots of it, soaking his paramilitary vest and coating his hands. 

A maniacal laugh, snapped Sola's attention back to Métier. Her lips, her face, her neck were smeared in thick, scarlet liquid  — Aster's blood. 

*Goddamn it! She tore open his throat with her teeth!* Sola's eyes widened in astonishment. She had seen Métier fight before, but never like this. At the moment the Inquirer was equally terrified and impressed by her old friend. 

"I can die happy now, having tasted the blood of you *metinik ashunta* bastard!" This was only the introduction to such a string of Silphite gutter profanity that Sola flushed. Mé's creativity was clearly not limited to the fiber arts. 

Whether it was the intensely scatological description of his, well, everything or the gaping neck wound, Aster was equally furious if much less creative. 

"You fucking bitch!" Aster began wildly kicking Métier in the ribs, head, arms, basically anywhere he could reach. Wild laughter continued to erupt out of the tailor for some time, but she eventually quieted into grunts. Soon there was only the dull thud of Aster's boots. An acrid smell filled the space and mixed with the metallic scent of blood. Aberseth had vomited upon himself. 

There was some kind of shrill shriek echoing through Sola's mind. She wasn't present exactly, instead somewhere beyond fear and fury. Her face was utterly expressionless when Aster, exhausted by exertion and blood loss, stumbling back and spun to face his sister. Two pairs of blue eyes met each other, one wild and one steely. 

"You had better kill me, Aster. You had better kill me this very instant. Or I am going to kill you." Her tone was flat but full of certainty.

It was Aster's turn to spit. He was still pressing a hand against the wound on his neck. "I see what you are doing.... what both of you are doing. No.... No more games." He was gasping for breath. "No more distractions... Give me the password... or I break her neck." He turned towards Aberseth. "Then I will go to work on you."

Sola continued to simply stare silently into her sibling's soul, but this was too much for the virologist. 

"Okay! Okay! I'll talk. Just... just... stop."

White hot fury rose in Sola. "You shut your mouth this —" Aster's foot shot out and struck Sola in the chest. First the wind was knocked out of her, then her chair tipped over and her head struck the hard floor, dazing her. She blurrily watched as Aster stepped in front of Aberseth.

"Continue."

"The password is —" 

For a moment Sola thought she had lost consciousness. A wave of vertigo and a loud roar swept over here before being replaced by a ringing noise. Her vision went dark for several interminable seconds. Then she felt hands working at her fetters. Then she was free. Meenshin's face swam into view. An arm tried to help her up but she pushed it away. She stumbled up and towards Métier, desperately and inarticulately praying to no one in particular. There was someone in the way. Jeanine. And others from Métier's crew. Sola shoved them aside and collapsed by her downed countrywoman. Others had already cut the tailor's binds. 

"Mé? Are you alive, Mé? Please. You have to be. You have to." A hand — Sola's hand — shakily peeled away hair matted with blood from Métier's face. She couldn't tell how much of the blood was Aster's and how much was... she couldn't think about that.

Métier's cool, gray eyes fluttered open. Or one of them anyways. The other was obscured by increasing swelling around her orbital. 

"Ah... my Inquisitor." Her voice veered wildly from gentle whimsy and moans of pain. "And I... didn't even ask for you to take on my case."

A raucous, full body expression of emotion exploded out of Sola's body, equal parts sobbing and laughter.

There was a hand on her shoulder. Words from Meenshin. Something about smoke and chemicals. They had to leave. Sola carefully hoisted Métier up to her feet. Some others tried to help but Sola snarled them away. 

Only now did Sola parse the scene through the smoke. The door was blown halfway across the room. If Sola hadn't been knocked down on the ground, it might have killed her. Some others were freeing Aberseth and helping him up. Plasticon, chemicals, and culture media were all on fire. Whatever fire suppression system this building had was clearly not operational. It was indeed time to get out of here while they still could.

***

The group limped out of the smoking wreck of a lab and headed a suitable distance down the path. Utopian citizens, well versed in the danger of uncontrolled fire, were already pouring past them with improvised dousing equipment. No doubt the Fire Safety Auxiliaries had already been notified and were on their way, even with the comms outage.

Sola was supporting most of Métier's weight, their cheeks pressed up against one another, smearing Aster's blood on both of them. The Inquirer's frenzied protectiveness was ebbing into relief.

"Mé?" Sola spoke softly, to an audience of one.

"Yes, my friend?" The tailor responded similarly gently.
 
"How the fuck did you manage to get kidnapped?"

Métier stiffened for a moment. "He tricked me." She said this as if it was the simplest thing in the world, but from the way she pointed that sharp chin of hers up and to the right told Sola that she was *deeply* embarrassed. 

"And how, pray tell, does one trick *the Madam Métier*?" Sola was trying and failing to suppress a smile, something that her companion could no doubt feel through their point of contact.

"The same as anyone else, Solaterra." Métier maintained her light, nonchalant tone that only partially masked the deep hurts to both her body and pride. "One part complete truth, and one part falsehood that the mark desperately wants to be true."

Sola was about to rebuke this crypticism when it suddenly clicked. Aster told Métier exactly who he was: Sola's imprisoned, revolutionary sibling. Long thought dead but now here, alive and looking to reunite with his estranged sister after all these years. The story was so thin as to be threadbare, suspect in the extreme. But to someone who cared deeply about Sola, it would be more than enough.

"Thanks, Mé." Sola pressed her cheek a bit harder into her friend's.

"For being saved by you?"

Sola let out a bark of a laugh. 

The pair staggered forward silently few a few paces, oblivious to the world around them. Then Métier spoke up.

"You know, you're one to talk. Your alias all these years was seriously just the first half of your given name?"

"It was easier to remember that way."

It was Métier's turn to laugh, a kind of quiet cackle. Then, after another silence. "My name is Len. Short for Selen."

Despite the labor of hauling the tailor down the path, Sola's breath caught in her thought. She had no idea how to handle two such expressions of vulnerability in so quick a succession. 

She settled for a simple, "Thanks, Len."

***

No more words passed between the women. Perhaps the battered tailor lapsed into unconsciousness from her injuries. Sola, exhausted and supporting the weight of two adults in heightened gravity, had to dedicate all her focus into simply putting one foot in front of another. The sum total of her desires now was simply to get Métier — Selen —far from that wretched warehouse and to somewhere safe.

Indeed, so focused was she on the basic process of locomotion that the two medics seemed to appear out of nowhere. They lifted Métier from the Inquirer's shoulders and easier the injured tailor to the ground. Only then did Sola become dimly aware of their surroundings once more. They were someways down the street, at a sharp curve forced by a Root that seemed to almost embrace the path. Sola slumped to the ground herself, sitting with her back against the rough, fiberous surface of the Roof. Its smell enveloped her, an earthy smell of soil and fungus and life. How refreshing it was after the impertinent artificiality of the smells in her recent environs. 

*How is it that a root floating free in space still smells like a root in loam?* This question hung idly in her mind while the almost sing-song, call-and-response of the medics' pitter-patter washed over her. At the best of times, she would have understood a small fraction of their queries, diagnoses, and prescriptions. At the moment, it was no different than bird song to the weary Inquirer.

Then Meenshin was sitting on her right, Aberseth on her left. The fugitive scientist seemed shell shocked, though it was unclear whether this was the result of the explosion or the events that had proceeded it. One of the medics detached from Métier and began moving a finger in front of the young man. Soon a penlight was being deployed.

"More than enough excitement for one day, eh lassie?" Meenshin sounded unperturbed by the proceedings.

Sola remained starring ahead at precisely nothing. She didn't have the energy to ask her neighobr what "lassie" meant. *One of his archaisms,* she imagined. *Or he simply made it up.* She judged these equally likely. 

Instead she answered with a more practical, though equally exhausted, question. "What the fuck just happened?"

Meenshin shrugged. "Got your message. Cute kid. Came running with some of the other lassie's crew. Doors were locked. Worried about hostages. So went to another Root garden a few doors down and borrowed some fertilizer."

Now Sola's eyes focused. She turned to look at her rescuer incredulously.

"You built a bomb?"

Meenshin shrugged again.

"You could have killed us!"

"But I didn't," he replied placidly.

"Since when do you know how to build a bomb?"

What the old man had pulled off was no joke. Making an impromptu bomb out of fertilizer was one thing — knowing how to direct the energy into the door as another. As was knowing how much fertilizer to use to get the job done without blowing the whole street into the the black.

"I wasn't always a gardener!" Meenshin seemed almost offended at Sola's skepticism "And your generation didn't invent direct action, I'll have you know."

Sola shook her head slowly in marvel, her gaze dropping to the ground in weariness.

"Where's the others? Jeanine? And what about Aster?"

"Aster? The fella who kidnapped you? He wasn't around when the smoke from the blast started to clear. The younguns went hauling off after him. Something about a trail of blood. I stuck around to look after you three."

"He'll be headed for Dirt Port. His ship is there." The Inquirer continued to make deductions, though her tone was lifeless.

"Good thing I sent Tomas to close the Port before we blew the door down." Meenshin was clearly pleased with himself. Sola knew Tomas, another revolutionary in Selen's orbit. "They may have done it already when the Rootside comms crapped out."

Some small relief crept down Sola's spine. The case wasn't over, not while Aster was still at large, but it was winding down. HE wasd injured, his escape cut off. Otherws could take it from here. It felt so good to be off her feet, to know that Selen — and Aberseth — were free and in good hands. She could take this moment to rest and relax.

*Then why can't I relax?* Something was nagging at her. Some piece in the puzzle wasn't quite fitting properly. Something that Meenshin just said had prompted the concern. No, that wasn't right. It was something that he had said earlier, back when this hole mess started.

*An emergency would be if the Invetlic's had a gunship parked up by the Crown.*

The Inquirer's head shot up, her eyes wide with frightful realization.

"He doesn't have to escape. He just has to reach his ship. Utopia's comms are down but he can still use his ship to signal to Beesheelon! "

*And in short order we'll face a full scale invasion. That's if they don't just decide to blow the station out of the void.*

Sola sprang to her feet. For the *n*th time today, adrenaline gave her some minor respite from the exhaustion and pain. She took all of two steps, before halting and swearing. She had just finished doing the mental math.

"I won't make it." Injured or not, Aster had too much of a headstart. If Selen's crew failed to track him down, there was nothing Sola could do.

"Course you will." Meenshin's warm and raspy old voice cut through her catastrophizing. "Just take the Root." Meenshin paternally patted the woody wall he was still sitting up against. "Third branch on the right, then second on the left."

Sola looked at the gardener incredulously yet again for just a moment. Then she was off, running for a spot a few meters down that looked more amenable to an ascent.

***
As she began her climb, hands reaching for tufts of rootstuff and feet scrambling for purchase, a host of reasons why this was a terrible idea ran through her head.

Climbing various parts of the Tree was a common enough hobby on Utopia, particularly for the more risk tolerant youths. The slopes of the Branches and Roots were highly variable, sometimes running almost parallel to a layer of the station (an uneven walk) and sometimes running almost perpendicular (an arduous and technical climb). This fact, coupled with the varying gravity of Utopia's layers, meant that it was relatively straightforward to select a route of arbitrary ease or difficulty. 

But Sola wasn't choosing her route now. She would be blind climbing an unfamiliar Root straight from the high gravity outer edge to just about the center of the station. She was an amateur climber at best and well past her prime. And she would be doing making the ascent without any safety gear.

There was also the fact that there was simply no way Meenshin had the course of this particular Root mapped out in his head. Sure he was a senior Root gardener, but that hardly required geographic knowledge of the entire Root system. This was an absurd endeavor likely to get her killed.

Sola paused her ascent for just a moment to glance down at the prone Selen some ways below her now, a reminder of why she was about to embark upon this folly. Meenshin's direction were probably some combination of overconfidence and senility. Sola almost certainly couldn't pull the climb off in time to stop Aster — or at all. But "probable" and "almost certain" demise were a helluva lot better odds than a full-scale Invetelic invasion. 

*Nothing for it. You gotta play to your outs.*

***

To Sola's immense and pleasurable surprise, the route was far easier than expected. Almost immediately, the near vertical climb of the initial stretch eased into a slope that, while steep, was significantly easier. the Inquirer was still using all four limbs against the high gravity, but she was able to find solid purchase on the rough and knobby texture of the Root with every reach. And if she did screw up, it would likely result in a short slide rather than a deadly fall.

Before she knew it, she had made it to the first split the old man had mentioned. Amazingly, this branch of the Root was at an even gentler slope than the last bit, enabling Sola to break into an almost hop-run that involved her arms, like those giant apes of Earth that she had once seen in a historical documentary. With every passing moment, the gravity eased still further.As a result, even though the second and final split curved truly vertical once more, the climb was trivial. A single arm was able to pull her entire weight up not the next toehold, but to the one after that, for she was throwing caution to the wind and leaping rather than climbing at this point.

And then she was hopping off the Root, drenched in sweat and gasping for breath. She didn't stop to rest though, instead immediately sprinting down the path that lead the last little ways to the entrance of Dirt Port.

The street here was much busier than out by the warehouse. Utopians and foreigners alike were on their way to and from the port, some carrying a simple backpack and other s guiding dollies — of the wheeled or hover varieties — laden with goods or personal belongings. Thankfully the crowd was not so dense as to meaningfully impede here progress. While the unusual sight someone so ragged and in such a hurry attracted more than a few stares and even a couple of offers of assistance, Sola was able to press. Soon she was at the entrance of the long, ramping tunnel that led into the massive metal disk that was the "bottom" of Utopia: the entrance of the Dirt Port. 

There was some yelling ahead, audible over the hubbub of the pedestrian traffic in that tunnel. There, further ahead, were two darkly clad figures in motion. One was Aster, doing an odd, wavering sprint upwards to the Port. The other, trailing behind, was a medic, no doubt expressing concern about the significant amount of blood Sola's brother was still leaving behind.

The Inquirer redoubled her efforts, her flanks heaving in desperate search for air. She sprinted past the entrance of the Port Engineers' workshop and then past the Quarantine Lab. She had closed the distance in half when Aster disappeared into the Port itself at the top of the ramp. The medic gave up the chance and shouted once more at their would-be patient impotently.

"Everyone's entitled to their own medical decisions, but you're a fucking moron!"

As Sola soon came to the top of the ramp herself, she saw Jack, having just exited the Port, bend down to step out of the magnetic boots used to traverse the minimal gravity floor of the Port. Sola immediately snapped the boots up and did a half-assed job of fastening while continuing to hop forward. Jack's feet were evidently a few sizes bigger than here, but Sola would hardly be picky at the moment. Jack started to protest but was stopped short by some combination of recognizing an Inquirer in haste (never a good sign), the frantic look in Sola's eyes, and the obscene mixture of Aster and Metíer's blood that covered her face and shirt.

Then Sola was out of the tunnel, the Port finally in view before her. A cylinder two hundred meters in diameter stretch upwards in both directions. Ships and people of diverse shape and sizes were scattered about the interior surface. Normally, the right wall would be open to space, protected only by a semi-permeable forcefield that let ships through but contained the atmosphere. At the moment, however, that view was sealed behind a telescoping metal barrier: the emergency gate. Evidently Meenshin's message had indeed reached the Port Engineers. 

*Too bad that won't matter if Aster gets a message off.*

Sola spotted her brother heading towards a decrepit old freighter of Xionian build. He had not stopped to don boots like she had and, as a result, was forced into a slow, lopping gait in the low gravity environment. Now was her chance. She briefly considered pulling a weapon. Her gun was lost back at the warehouse, inside the coat Aster had taken from her. The baton had felt missing as well, but she might still have her knuckles. She discarded the thought instantly though. The same mother had taught both of them how to conceal weapons. Aster would doubtless have found and taken her knuckles and even her pocket knife. Armed with just her wits and her fury, Sola resumed the pursuit.

The ill-fitting magnetic boots clanged with each step. As she grew closer to her quarry, that sound evidently reached his ears when he was only a few meters off from the waiting boarding ramp of his ship. Aster half turned to look behind him. She saw the eyes they had in common grow wide with fear on a pallid face. His left hand was awkwardly clasped on his neck wound, only inadequately stemming the bleeding.  The half-turn caused him to lose his balance and stumble slowly in the low gravity. The Inquirer took the opportunity to gain more ground. Only a few meters more.

But then Aster recovered his balance. Sola saw his right hand reach into a pocket and pull out a gun, shiny and dark in the Port's bright lights.

Out of time and out of options, Sola flung herself at her target, desperately willing herself to make the distance. Her boots, too large and poorly fastened, remained fixed on the floor as the Inquirer flew through the air. She slammed into Aster hard, much harder than she had anticipated, only barely managing to maintain a grip on him. The siblings went flying in a tangled mass, grappling for advantage.

Sharp pain blossomed in Sola's left side when she slammed into one of the landing struts of Aster's ship. The impact on the angled surface redirected their flight and sent the pair spinning up and away from the floor, towards the center of the Port.

They continued to fight in that microgravity environment. This was not the close quarters combat that Sola had been trained for in police academy and Aster by the Invetelic Secret Service. This was not even the street brawling that their parents had taught them and that Sola had refined dealing with rowdy smugglers. The two were injured, exhausted, panicked, and fighting in an environment neither had any meaningful experience with. What ensued could generously be called semi-intentional flailing. Sola was trying to bind her brother's right arm before he could bring the gun to bear on her. Aster trashed and kicked and punched, desperately trying to free himself from his younger sibling.

Sola's world was a whirlwind of motion, dark cloth, blue eyes, and pale skin slick with blood. 

The gun went off with a bang more felt than heard. A projectile slammed hard into Sola's body. Then everything went black.

***

Sola's eyes opened slowly to a world made blurry by yet another raging headache. Her back stung in three distinct spots and her left side was tender. She had woken up like this enough times to recognize the Medical Cooperative's clinic cots by feel, even though she couldn't see for shit yet.

*Goddam. I almost* wish *the Port's suppressors were lethal  if this is what they feel like.* A prolonged groan wound its way up from her belly and out her mouth.

"Ah, my dear Inquisitor awakens." Selen's lilt, a merciful balm in world of groggy pain, drew Sola's attention to the left. There her eyes gradually focused on the tailor, who was sitting upright in another clinic cot, reading a book. Her left arm was in a cast, but her right arm seemed a tad more functional. She closed her book and laid it on her lap with a theatrical grace that was immediately undermined by the sharp wince of pain from the movement. Either the medic's were being stingy with the painkillers or she had decline them for some personal reason.

The blood had been cleaned up from Selen's face and chest at some point though. What was uncovered was swollen mass of turning bruises and a likely broken nose, already reset by expert hands. No doubt the rest of her body tooked similar under the clinic blanket and gown. A dull rage stirred in Sola.

"You look like shit, Len." 

This was met with an arched eyebrow. "At least it is only a temporary condition for me, my dear."

Sola let out a bark of a laugh that directly transitioned into another groan. 

"Who's the medic on beat?" Any chance of convincing them to give us a bottle of *tequipa*? Or at least a decent cup of *mahtar*?"

"To our great misfortune, our attending is Carol, so good luck. I already used all my charms to obtain a glass of *vimona*, to no avail. If she didn't surrender to me, what chance do you have?"

"I thought anarchism meant no rules." This joke so so exceedingly old on Utopia that it had crossed back over into being funny again. That or Selen's peal of laughter was the result of pity. Either way, it was a delight to Sola's ears.

"Don't worry, next time Tomas or Jeanine come by, they'll bring something."

Sola sighted and laid back on her pillow. It was only then she noticed the cot to her right. She had known there was a cot there, of course. Almost all the patient rooms in the emergency clinic had four and there was an empty one just past Selen. Rather, the Inquirer now noticed that the remaining bed was in fact occupied.

Their fellow patient was sleeping or unconscious, their chest slowly rising and falling. Thick, medicated bandages obscured most of their face neck — the parts she could see from her reclined position, at least. What betrayed his identity where the restraints binding him to the bed. Not the metal cuffs used by the police of Sola's past life. She doubted there was a single pain of such cuffs anywhere on the station (outside of some perhaps recreational sets). These were the padded cloth restraints used to secure a patient not in their right mind.

*Aster.* A deep emptiness opened up in Sola's chest as the events just before her incapacitation settled back over her. *My brother, alive and would-be murderer of millions.*

Selen's eyes followed her companion's gaze. "Ah. Yes. *Him*." The pronouns were uttered with great and justified malice. Evidently recalling the likely more conflicting emotions Sola was likely experiencing, she backpedaled. "I mean... sorry, dear... I know this must —"

"What are we going to do with him?" Sola wasn't looking for sympathy at the moment so instead for focused on practical matters. Utopia had no prisons. Instead the station had a variety of community-based reconciliation processes for the more serious ethical violations by residents. For outsiders, there was an informal exile, implemented by whoever was on hand and generally respected by residents and regular traders alike. Sola didn't think either was appropriate in this situation.

Part of her wanted to deny responsibility for what happened next. That was how it had been as an Invetelic detective. Solve the mystery, catch the criminal, hand them off to prosecutors or the secret police, move on to the next case. Of course that mentality was precisely what had led to this situation, as well as to innumerable injustices along the way. When reluctantly taking on the mantle of Inquirer, Sola had sworn to herself that this would never again be way she worked. She would be personally and morally involved in the resolution of each case, in ensuring that real justice was found and that this justice was balanced with mercy and the possibility of healing. The problem was, she had no idea what that would like here.

Apparently neither did Selen. "No clue. Probably months of community meetings to figure that one out. There's no hurry at the moment though. He's in a coma. Some combination of the Port suppressors, a head injury from Meenshin's suprisingly effective improvised explosives, and... " She trailed off.

"Blood loss from the neck wound?" Sola was unsure how to feel about all this. Generally shitty, she supposed. She also supposed that she should be feeling... well *more* than she currently was.

"Yes." Slen was clearly feeling more than a bit complicated about the matter herself. "I'm afraid I... well... did quite a number on him... I suppose." She sighed. "The medics aren't sure when he'll wake up. Or if." A long pause. "I'm sorry, Sol. This is fucked up."

Sola sighed heavily and looked back up at the ceiling. "Yeah, I... I don't know, Len." She was still getting used to that name, the shape of it on her tongue. "All I know for sure is... I'm grateful you're alive. I was... so scared..." It was Sola's turn to trail off uncomfortably.

Selen reached out across the gap between cots and placed a gentle hand on her friend's shoulder, grimacing in pain slightly at the effort.

"That might be the sweetest thing you've ever said to me, my Solaterra."

Sola broke into sobs.

***

The next morning found Sola's bed pushed up against Selen's. The two were both sitting up the joint head of the cots, the tailor's hand resting on the Inquirer's shoulder. Carol had nominally discharged Sola the previous evening. While getting knocked unconscious twice in one day is certainly rough on the body, Sola's injuries were relatively minor and didn't require ongoing attention from a medic. The problem was, the only place Sola *wanted* to go was the Branched Path Tavern and she was altogether too familiar with the dangers of drinking alone. She had spent a good portion of her first couple of years on Utopia in one bar or another doing precisely that. It took Selen metaphorically and literally grabbing her by the collar and hauling her back to her feet before she broke out of that loop. This time around, Sola decided to skip straight to the heart-to-heart with Selen.

The two had spent most of hte night talking over the situation and Sola's past, all while passing back and forth a handle of *tequipa* that Tomas had smuggled in for them. This was Sola's chance to tell her version of the story, rather than Aster's. Occasionally, one or both of them would momentarily drift off to sleep, not that this interrupted the conversation.

"I don't expect you to forgive me for all that I did." This was not the first time Sola had said some variant of this during the conversation.

Selen was silent for a long while, her breathing so soft and regular against Sola's shoulder, that the Inquirer thought her companion might have fallen asleep again. But then she spoke, her voice muffled by Sola's gown.

"I haven't decided whether to forgive you yet. But I'm glad to know the truth finally. I'm glad you got away. And I'm glad you're still alive."

"I can live with that. I'll have to —"

At that moment, two people entered the room and Sola deftly hid the *tequipa*. One was the medic Carol, who smirked to see the two of her patients seated together. She said nothing , however and simply began checking vitals, first on Selen and then on the unconscious Aster. The other visitor was Akiné, a young Volunteer Diplomat who looked every bit the part. A short, dense beard of dark, curly hair somehow managed to accentuate his elegant jawline rather than hide it. A delicate golden tree hung from one ear, contrasting nicely with the deep brown of his skin. He wore a lovely black dress thickly embroidered with floral patterns of black thread (all made by Madam Métier) and his feet were clad in matching black sandals. Not unlike Carol, his brown eyes sparkled with mischievous joy upon seeing the pair.

Akiné strode across the small room and seated himself gracefully upon the foot of Sola's cot. His hands smoothed the dress with the instinct born of experience. Then he shot them a dazzlingly bright smile.

"Good morning, neighbors." His voice was, as it had been every time they had previously met, deeper  than Sola expected, comforting and reassuring. "Madam Métier, Inquirer Sola, I hope you are recovering quickly in the lovely Carol's care?"

Carol, bent over the unconscious Aster, rolled her eyes at this. Selen raised her head from Sola's shoulder and gave a resigned shrug.

"We suffer what we must for the cause, I'm afraid," she said nonchalantly. "I am simply grateful my hands are unscathed. It would be a terrible shame if I could no longer make such pretty dresses for you, darling Akiné. You appreciate my work so much more than this one." She dismissively gestured at Sola. "She managed to lose the coat I so generously made her. Careless."

It was Sola's turn to roll her eyes. Akiné chose not to acknowledge the dig. 

"I and all of Utopia are grateful your hands are intact."

The Inquirer had about enough of this. "I assume you didn't come all the way down from the Crown to inquire about your tailor's health?"

Akiné smiled again. "Ah, Inquirer Sola, ever straight to the point. Indeed I came here because the Diplomats thought you might want to hear about how your most recent case shook out." Sola's instant focus was all the confirmation he needed. "Shortly after the apprehension of the unfortunate Aster Trabahan of Silphus..." The diplomat inclined his head respectfully towards the comatose figure, "... we were able to re-open the Dirt Port. Thankfully the envoys of our Invetelic neighbors were stationed off the Sky Port and noticed nothing amiss.

"The young scientist Aberseth Hamshell and his brilliant but disturbing work, found on Aster's person, were sent on to safer harbors." Akiné placed a closed fish over his heart. "The Diplomats thanks your colleagues, Madam Métier, for their willingness to adjust their time tables so."

Selen waved a hand dismissively. "Our duty, nothing more."

"Young Aberseth asked us to relay his gratitude to both of you for his protection during his stay here."

Selen's eyebrow arched skeptically. Sola's tips tightened in shame and embarrassment. Arsiné politely ignored both reactions.

"This morning, three warships flying Invetelic colors exited the Ring and stationed themselves off the Sky Port." Arkiné said this calmly, as if it was an everyday occurrence, but both Selen and Sola stiffened in alarm. "*Seela* Beesheelon opened communication channels and informed us that —" the Diplomat switched into a tone that resembled the Bayhatile's remarkably well. "Troops of the Immortal Invetlic Empire will soon begin landing at Sky Port. They shall conduct a search of Utopia for a dangerous fugitive. As subjects of the Immortal Empire, you are obligated to facilitate their search. Any opposition or interference will be considered an act of treason and dealt with accordingly. We thank you for your compliance."

Both patients rolled their eyes at this. Carol, who had finished up the morning exam but was clearly lingering in order to hear the story, chuckled scornfully. Akiné smiled at the reaction, evidently relishing the rapt audience.

"We responded that, as an independent polity, we are under no such obligations. Furthermore, we had evidence that an agent of the Invetelic intelligence service, who had assaulted multiple Utopian citizens, was using researched conducted in the lab of one *Seela* Beesheelon to develop and deploy a bioweapon against the Xionians." The diplomat paused for effect. Carol was no longer pretending to anything other than listen.

Akiné took a breath, then continued. "After a several minute wait, *Seela* Beesheelon declared that, on behalf of their glorious empire, they were willing to offer Utopia full autonomy in exchange for refraining from sharing that information. We replied that this sounded like a perfectly equitable exchange—"

The room erupted in protest.

"We can't just sell —!"

"— could kill entire —!"

"— a betrayal of our revolutionary ideals!"

Akiné raised his hands to gesture for silence. After a few moments, the dissent quieted, overtaken by morbid curiosity.

"*Unfortunately*, *Seela* Beesheelon had misunderstood us. We had *already* shared the evidence with one other polity and word would soon reach several others. We were not attempting to *bargain* with the *Seela*, we were instead providing a friendly warning to our neighbor. Our understanding is that the Invetelic Empire would not look kindly upon the *Seela's* management of research security. We recommended that *Seela* Beesheelon run fast and fly hard." 

Akiné allowed a dramatic silence to elapse before ending with aplomb. "*Seela* Beesheelon's ship promptly Switched. The three warships soon followed in pursuit. Speaking in my personal capacity, I am not optimistic for our recent guest's chances."

The anger in the room instantly evaporated into celebratory laughter. Selen was soon proposing a toast and Sola was pouring *tequipa* into small cups provided by Carol, who's earlier opposition to patient alcohol consumption had apparently gone by the wayside.

"To the indefatigably of the revolutionary spirit that sustains our struggle and to the contradictions in our oppressors that provide for their annihilation." 

Sola drank deeply and silently added,"We thank them for their compliance."
